1. Punishment Theory (Deterrence, Retribution, Rehabilitation, Incapacitation)

Doctrine Overview

Criminal punishment must serve legitimate state goals. The major theories justifying punishment are:

  • Deterrence: Preventing crime by making punishment costly. Specific deterrence affects the offender; general deterrence affects the public.
  • Retribution: Punishment is deserved based on moral culpability. Proportionality required.
  • Rehabilitation: Reform offender through programs; limited proven effectiveness.
  • Incapacitation: Physically prevent offender from committing further crimes through imprisonment.

Key Elements & Distinctions

Deterrence: Assumes rational actors calculate costs/benefits. Effectiveness depends more on certainty than severity of punishment. Example: If 100% chance of $100 fine, stronger deterrent than 10% chance of $1000 fine.

Retribution: Backward-looking: "What did defendant do?" Not forward-looking consequences. Punishment is intrinsically justified, not merely consequentialist. Must be finite and proportionate.

Rehabilitation: Forward-looking: "Can we reform this person?" Individual variation means effectiveness unpredictable. Risk: Indeterminate sentences that may be unfair.

Incapacitation: Forward-looking: "How dangerous is this person?" Effective but costly. Requires imperfect risk prediction.

Key Cases

Ewing v. California, 538 U.S. 11 (2003)
Facts: Defendant shoplifted videotape; third strike conviction resulted in life sentence.
Holding: Life sentence for shoplifting not grossly disproportionate; three strikes law constitutional.
Rule: Both retribution and incapacitation are legitimate penological goals justifying harsh sentences. Proportionality review is very deferential.
Significance for exam: Establishes that backward-looking (retribution) and forward-looking (incapacitation) rationales can both justify severe sentences. Shows courts support multiple punishment theories simultaneously.

Graham v. Florida, 560 U.S. 48 (2010)
Holding: Juvenile life without parole unconstitutional except for homicide offenses.
Rule: Proportionality principles apply; juveniles have diminished culpability limiting incapacitation rationale.
Significance: Shows limits on incapacitation theory when applied to less culpable actors.

Miller v. Alabama, 567 U.S. 460 (2012)
Holding: Mandatory life without parole for juveniles unconstitutional.
Rule: Individual mitigation and character required; one-size-fits-all sentences impermissible even for serious crimes.
Significance: Individualized sentencing required; retribution theory must account for personal characteristics.

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM. Likely as context for sentencing and proportionality issues in homicide questions, not as standalone topic.

Hypothetical: Rick and Morty - Punishment Theories in Action

Rick has been arrested for grand theft auto (stealing a spaceship). He has a prior conviction for the same crime 15 years ago. The judge must decide on sentencing and must articulate which punishment theories justify the sentence.

ANALYSIS:

Deterrence argument (judge might impose moderate sentence, e.g., 2 years): Rick is a repeat offender, suggesting past punishment failed to specifically deter him. But general deterrence may still work if the public sees he faces real consequences. However, if Rick is highly intelligent and calculating risk, certainty matters more than severity—a guaranteed 2-year sentence might deter more than uncertain 10-year sentence.

Retribution argument (judge might impose proportionate sentence, e.g., 3 years): How culpable is Rick morally? Stealing a spaceship is serious property crime. His prior conviction shows he knew the punishment and did it anyway, possibly showing greater moral blameworthiness. Retribution says punishment should match moral desert—not too lenient, not disproportionately harsh.

Rehabilitation argument (judge might recommend programs and shorter sentence, e.g., 18 months + rehabilitation): Can Rick be reformed? If he's willing to participate in vocational programs or counseling, rehabilitation theory might justify shorter sentence with conditions. But if Rick is incapable of rehabilitation (shown by repeat offense), this theory fails.

Incapacitation argument (judge might impose longer sentence, e.g., 5 years): How dangerous is Rick to society? If he's highly likely to steal again (evidenced by prior conviction), incapacitation justifies longer sentence to prevent future crimes. If there's evidence Rick will definitely reoffend, incapacitation trumps other theories.

EXAM ISSUE: The judge could combine theories. E.g., "I sentence Rick to 3 years (retribution for his moral culpability + general deterrence to warn others) with a requirement to complete auto-theft awareness program (rehabilitation) because testing shows he's not an incorrigible repeat offender."

2. Actus Reus (Voluntary Act, Omissions, Possession, Status Crimes)

Doctrine Overview

Actus Reus = the guilty act; the physical/external element of crime. Fundamental principle: No crime without an act (nullum crimen sine actu).

Constitutional requirement: Criminal liability must be based on voluntary act, not mere thoughts or status. Prevents thought crimes and protects due process.

The Voluntary Act Requirement

Test: Was the act the product of the defendant's conscious will and effort?

What counts: Bodily movements (including speaking words when required), possession, omissions (in limited cases).

What doesn't count: Involuntary movements (seizures, reflexes, sleepwalking), status alone, thoughts without accompanying conduct.

Examples:

  • D shoots victim intentionally = voluntary act
  • D's arm swings reflexively and strikes victim = involuntary (reflex)
  • D speaks threats while sleepwalking = involuntary (no conscious will)
  • D experiences seizure and commits violence during seizure = involuntary

Omission Liability (Failure to Act)

General Rule: No duty to act; failure to act cannot form basis of criminal liability. Criminal law protects against affirmative harm, not failure to prevent harm.

Exceptions - Duty to Act arises from:

  • Special Relationship: Parent-child, spouse-spouse (in some jurisdictions), caretaker-dependent. Parent must provide food, shelter, medical care to child.
  • Contractual Duty: Lifeguard, nurse, security guard. Lifeguard hired to rescue swimmers has duty.
  • Voluntary Undertaking: If D begins rescue, must continue. Cannot abandon halfway. Creates duty to others in similar position.
  • Dangerous Condition Creation: If D creates dangerous condition, duty to remedy arises. D leaves trash blocking fire exit; fire spreads because exit blocked.
  • Statutory Duty: Statutes may impose duty to act. Failure to register as sex offender, failure to file taxes.

Key Case: Omission Liability

Commonwealth v. Welansky, 355 Mass. 585 (1969)
Facts: Nightclub owner; fire broke out; inadequate exits and blocked exit led to 490 deaths.
Holding: Owner had duty based on creating the dangerous condition (inadequate exits).
Rule: When defendant creates dangerous condition, failure to remedy can constitute criminal negligence based on omission.
Significance: Shows omission liability is possible when defendant bears responsibility for dangerous condition.

Possession

Definition: Knowing possession with intent to exercise control.

Elements:

  • Knowledge of the existence of the object
  • Knowledge of its incriminating character
  • Conscious dominion and control (direct or constructive)
  • Intent to exercise control (can be inferred)

Direct possession: Immediate control (in hand, on person).

Constructive possession: Indirect control (hidden in home, in vehicle with access).

Joint possession: Multiple people can jointly possess same item.

Mere presence insufficient: Being present where contraband exists does not equal possession without dominion and control.

Key Case: Status Crimes

Robinson v. California, 370 U.S. 660 (1962)
Facts: California law criminalized being addicted to drugs.
Holding: Unconstitutional to criminalize mere status.
Rule: Criminal liability requires conduct element; status alone insufficient due process violation and cruel punishment.
Significance: Fundamental principle: actus reus requirement prevents status crimes. Can criminalize conduct related to status (possession of drugs, being under influence while driving) but not status itself.

Exam Likelihood: HIGH. Expect questions on voluntary act, omission duty, and possession elements. Sexual assault scenarios often test omission and consent issues.

Hypothetical: Family Guy - Actus Reus and Omission Duty

Peter Griffin, drunk, passes out on the couch. His toddler Stewie finds a bottle of Peter's prescription painkillers on the coffee table (left there because Peter was too lazy to put them away), opens it, and swallows several pills. Lois finds Stewie unresponsive two hours later. Stewie is rushed to hospital and survives after stomach pumping and treatment. Peter is charged with child endangerment through reckless omission and negligent failure to secure dangerous items.

ANALYSIS - Voluntary Act Issue: Peter didn't actively poison Stewie; he left pills accessible. Is there an actus reus? Yes, through omission. Peter's conduct = failure to secure known dangerous item he created the risk of.

Analysis - Omission Duty Issue: Does Peter have duty to act (i.e., to secure pills)? YES. Special relationship between parent and child creates affirmative duty. Peter must take reasonable steps to protect Stewie from known dangers.

Analysis - Breach of Duty: What was Peter's duty? As parent, Peter has duty to:

  • Provide safe environment for child
  • Secure dangerous items (prescription drugs)
  • Supervise child appropriately
Did Peter breach it? YES. He left pills accessible, didn't supervise, and was intoxicated unable to respond if problem arose.

Analysis - Mens Rea Match: Charge is "reckless omission" or "negligent failure." Peter:

  • Was aware of risk? (Recklessness) - Arguably yes, Peter knows painkillers are dangerous
  • Consciously disregarded risk? (Recklessness) - Questionable; Peter was passed out, so maybe not "conscious"
  • Should have been aware of risk? (Negligence) - Clearly yes; reasonable parent would realize pills accessible to toddler is dangerous

Likely outcome: Negligent failure to secure (objective standard) more likely than reckless omission (requires consciousness). Peter's omission (failure to secure) satisfies actus reus requirement via special relationship.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Can omission constitute actus reus? Yes, with duty. (2) When does duty arise? Special relationship with child. (3) Does intoxication negate voluntary act? Generally no; recklessness/negligence can still apply. (4) Could Peter argue no voluntary act because he was intoxicated? Weak argument; the omission was his failure to act sober.

3. Mens Rea (Purpose, Knowledge, Recklessness, Negligence, Transferred Intent, Willful Blindness)

Doctrine Overview

Mens Rea = guilty mind; the mental state/intent required for criminal liability. No crime without mens rea (in most cases). Different crimes require different mental states.

MPC Four Levels of Culpability (Section 2.02): Purpose, Knowledge, Recklessness, Negligence.

Purpose (Highest Culpability)

Definition: Conscious objective to engage in conduct or cause result.

Test: Did defendant consciously want to cause the result?

Examples:

  • D intends to kill V and shoots V = acts with purpose to kill
  • D intends to steal property and takes it = purpose to steal
  • D seeks to cause serious bodily injury = purpose to injure

Key: Timing matters. Intent must exist at time of conduct. If D shoots V intending only to scare (not kill), later V dies = no purpose to kill, though may be recklessness/negligence.

Knowledge (High Culpability)

Definition: Awareness that conduct is practically certain to cause the result.

Test: Did defendant know (with practical certainty) that result would occur?

Subjective standard: Requires actual awareness, not what reasonable person would know.

Examples:

  • D knows gun is loaded and shoots at V's head from 2 feet away = acts knowingly regarding death
  • D knows driving 100 mph through school zone creates substantial risk = knowingly creates risk
  • Drug smuggler who KNOWS package contains drugs = knows of incriminating character

Recklessness (Moderate Culpability)

Definition: Conscious disregard for substantial risk; grossly deviates from conduct of ordinary person.

Elements:

  • Awareness of substantial risk (conscious awareness)
  • Conscious disregard of that risk (deliberate choice to ignore)
  • Risk must be substantial (not mere possibility)
  • Gross deviation from ordinary person's conduct

Examples:

  • D drives 50 mph through residential area at 2 AM, aware someone might be injured = reckless
  • D shoots gun in direction where people might be = reckless (if D knew of risk)
  • D drives with known faulty brakes = reckless (aware of risk, disregards it)

Key distinction from knowledge: Recklessness = aware of substantial risk, not practical certainty. Knowledge = practically certain result will occur.

Negligence (Lowest Culpability)

Definition: Unaware of risk but should be aware; failure to perceive substantial risk that reasonable person would perceive.

Elements:

  • Objective standard (what reasonable person would perceive)
  • Risk must be substantial
  • Failure to perceive is gross deviation from reasonable conduct
  • No consciousness requirement (differs from recklessness)

Examples:

  • D doesn't see pedestrian and hits them (reasonable person would have seen) = negligent
  • D leaves gun on coffee table and child finds it = negligent (reasonable parent wouldn't)
  • D drives with defective brakes, doesn't know they're defective = negligent (should have known)

Key: Defendant doesn't have to know of risk; reasonable person standard applies.

Transferred Intent Doctrine

Rule: Intent to harm one person may transfer to actual victim if harm is same type.

Same-crime transfers:

  • D intends to kill A, kills B = intent to kill transfers; D guilty of murdering B
  • D intends to assault A, assaults B = intent transfers
  • D intends to burn A's house, accidentally burns B's house = intent transfers

Different-crime transfers: Generally DO NOT transfer

  • D intends to rape B, kills A in process = intent to rape doesn't transfer to murder
  • D intends to commit theft, accidentally causes injury = intent to steal doesn't transfer to assault

Limitation: Applies to acts and intents, not to elements of harm/result type.

Willful Blindness

Definition: Deliberately avoiding knowledge counts as "knowledge" for mens rea purposes.

Requirements:

  • Defendant must suspect the truth
  • Defendant must deliberately avoid confirming suspicion
  • Defendant's motive is irrelevant

Examples:

  • Drug smuggler who doesn't want to know what's in package = has knowledge anyway
  • Fence who deliberately doesn't ask details about stolen goods = has knowledge
  • Person who deliberately avoids investigating suspicious circumstances = has knowledge

Key: Must be deliberate/conscious avoidance, not mere negligence or failure to investigate.

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Expect multiple questions testing mental state analysis. Transferred intent and willful blindness commonly tested in essays.

Hypothetical: Ted (Mark Wahlberg) - Mens Rea Gradations and Transferred Intent

Ted, an adult teddy bear who can talk, gets into a heated argument with his human best friend John over a woman. In blind rage, Ted says, "I'm going to beat the crap out of you," grabs a baseball bat, and swings it wildly at John. John ducks, and the bat hits Tom Brady (a stranger walking by) in the head, fracturing his skull. Tom Brady is hospitalized. Ted is charged with assault with intent to cause serious bodily injury (counts as purpose/knowledge of injury), and in the alternative, reckless assault.

ANALYSIS - Mens Rea for Intent to Injure John:

Ted clearly acted with PURPOSE to cause serious bodily injury to John. He: (1) said he'd beat John, (2) grabbed bat (implement of violence), (3) swung at close range at John's body. This demonstrates conscious objective to cause serious injury. PURPOSE (highest mens rea) satisfied for John.

ANALYSIS - Transferred Intent to Tom Brady:

Can Ted's intent to injure John transfer to Tom Brady? YES. This is same-crime transfer: intent to commit assault on person A transfers to actual victim (person B) when same type of harm occurs. So Ted is liable for assault with intent to cause serious bodily injury to Tom Brady even though Ted never thought of Tom Brady.

ANALYSIS - Why Transferred Intent Works Here: The harm type is identical (serious bodily injury via baseball bat), so justice is satisfied by holding Ted liable for assaulting Tom Brady with same intent he had for John. The "intent to cause serious injury" is the mens rea element, and it transferred.

ANALYSIS - Alternative: Reckless Assault on Tom Brady:

If court rejects transferred intent (some jurisdictions limit it), could Ted be guilty of reckless assault on Tom Brady? YES. Ted:

  • Was aware of substantial risk? YES. Swinging bat wildly creates awareness that innocent bystanders might be hit
  • Consciously disregarded that risk? ARGUABLY YES. Ted didn't look around; swung bat without checking if others were nearby
  • Grossly deviated from ordinary conduct? YES. Ordinary person doesn't swing bats wildly in public places
So even without transferred intent, recklessness covers the assault on Tom Brady.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) How to establish purpose/intent, (2) Transferred intent doctrine and when it applies (same-crime transfers yes; different crimes generally no), (3) Fallback to recklessness when intent doesn't transfer, (4) Risk awareness and conscious disregard for recklessness. Also teaches that even wild swings in public are analyzed for recklessness as to all foreseeable victims.

4. Causation (But-For, Proximate Cause, Intervening Causes)

Doctrine Overview

Causation Requirement: Defendant's conduct must have caused the prohibited result. Two distinct requirements: (1) cause-in-fact (but-for causation), and (2) proximate/legal cause.

Principle: Result crimes (homicide, assault causing injury) require both actual causation and legal causation.

Cause-in-Fact (But-For Causation)

Test: "But for" the defendant's conduct, would the result have occurred?

Counterfactual: What would have happened without the defendant's act?

Application:

  • "But for" D pulling trigger, V would not have died = D is cause-in-fact
  • "But for" D's failure to report fire, deaths would have occurred anyway = No cause-in-fact (failure to act didn't cause result that was going to happen anyway)

Burden: Prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that result wouldn't have occurred "but for" defendant's conduct.

Problems with But-For Test

Overdetermination: When multiple causes independently sufficient for result (two shooters simultaneously fire fatal shots at victim). Solutions vary: Some courts hold both caused it (independently sufficient); some hold neither did (only one could be necessary); some apply alternative causes doctrine.

Acceleration of Inevitable Result: V has terminal illness and will die in 1 month; D murders V, accelerating death by 1 month. Most jurisdictions: D still caused death (accelerating result counts as causation).

Impossibility: D gives V poison; V dies in car crash moments later. D's poison didn't cause death; conduct couldn't possibly cause result. No causation.

Proximate (Legal) Cause

Definition: Normative question of whether fairness and policy justify holding defendant liable for the result. Limits scope of but-for causation to foreseeable results.

Function: Prevents liability for remote, unforeseeable, bizarre results.

Tests Used (varies by jurisdiction):

  • Foreseeability Test: Was type of harm foreseeable? Was manner foreseeable?
  • Direct Cause Test: Was result direct consequence without intervening acts?
  • Scope of Risk Test: Was result within scope of risk that conduct created?

Intervening Causes

Definition: An act that occurs AFTER defendant's conduct but BEFORE the result.

Two Types:

A. Dependent Intervening Cause

  • Depends on or is responsive to defendant's conduct
  • Rule: Defendant typically REMAINS LIABLE
  • Example: D leaves V bleeding; V tries to flag down car and is hit = dependent (responding to injury)
  • Example: D threatens V; V flees and is hit = dependent (fleeing in response to threat)
  • Example: D poisons V; V seeks medical care and dies from medication interaction = dependent (seeking help in response)

B. Independent Intervening Cause

  • Independent of and unrelated to defendant's conduct
  • Rule: Defendant's liability is BROKEN by independent intervening cause
  • Example: D negligently injures V; murderer kills V in hospital = independent (third-party crime, not foreseeable)
  • Example: Criminal third-party acts generally break causation (unless foreseeable)
  • Example: Victim's voluntary suicide typically breaks causation (conscious will intervenes)

Key Cases

Commonwealth v. Root, 403 Pa. 571 (1960)
Facts: D fled from police at high speed on public road; another car hit and killed V (pedestrian).
Holding: Jury question whether D's conduct was proximate cause; depends on foreseeability of other driver's conduct.
Rule: Other drivers' conduct can be independent intervening cause breaking chain if not foreseeable.
Significance: Direct cause test vs. foreseeability test; shows other drivers' decisions may break causation.

Commonwealth v. Carter, 52 Mass. App. Ct. 692 (1982)
Facts: D encouraged girlfriend to jump from building; she did; she died.
Holding: No proximate causation; victim's voluntary, conscious decision breaks chain.
Rule: Victim's independent will to commit suicide breaks causation chain even if D encouraged it.
Significance: Shows limits on D's liability when victim makes conscious, voluntary choice.

Exam Likelihood: HIGH. Expect causation questions in homicide scenarios, especially testing intervening causes and proximate cause limits.

Hypothetical: South Park - Causation and Intervening Causes

Cartman is angry at Kyle for humiliating him. Cartman spreads vicious lies about Kyle at school, telling everyone Kyle is a "rat" who snitched to authorities. Kyle is devastated by the social ostracism and spends nights crying. Kyle's mental health deteriorates. Three months later, Kyle attempts suicide by jumping off a bridge. Kyle survives with serious injuries (broken legs, spinal damage). Kyle sues Cartman (civil), and state considers criminal charges against Cartman for assault/battery or reckless endangerment.

ANALYSIS - Causation Questions:

But-For Causation: "But for" Cartman's lies, would Kyle have jumped off bridge? Possibly, but it's debatable. Kyle might have jumped anyway if other factors (depression, bullying, mental illness) were independent causes. Some might say: Yes, Cartman's lies were the "but for" cause because without them, Kyle wouldn't have been in that psychological state. Others: No, because Kyle made autonomous choice to jump; his mental state and that choice are independent.

Proximate Causation Issue - Intervening Cause Analysis:

Even if but-for causation exists, was Kyle's decision to jump an intervening cause that breaks proximate causation?

  • Is Kyle's suicide decision dependent or independent? Kyle's decision is responsive to Cartman's conduct (Kyle jumped because of emotional harm from lies), making it arguably dependent.
  • Foreseeability: Was it foreseeable that vicious lies would cause psychological harm leading to suicide attempt? YES, reasonably foreseeable that extreme bullying/ostracism causes serious emotional harm and could lead to self-harm.
  • Scope of Risk: Was suicide within scope of risk Cartman's lies created? YES. Cartman created risk of severe emotional harm, which includes risk of self-harm.
  • Voluntary Act vs. Intervening Cause: Kyle's jump is a voluntary act, but unlike cases where victim voluntarily commits suicide after careful deliberation (breaking causation), here Kyle's suicide attempt was direct response to psychological injury (making it dependent).

Likely Outcome: Under modern/foreseeability test, proximate cause probably EXISTS because: (1) Kyle's self-harm was foreseeable consequence of extreme bullying, (2) Kyle's act was dependent/responsive to Cartman's conduct, (3) Scope of risk included psychological harm and self-harm.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) But-for causation (was Cartman's conduct necessary for Kyle's state?), (2) Independent vs. dependent intervening cause (does Kyle's voluntary decision break causation?), (3) Foreseeability and scope of risk as limits on proximate cause, (4) Distinction between intervening cause that breaks causation (third-party murder) vs. one that doesn't (victim's foreseeable response).

5. Homicide Overview (Murder vs. Manslaughter Hierarchy)

Doctrine Overview

Homicide = unlawful killing of human being. Divided into two main categories:

  • Murder: Unlawful killing with malice aforethought (highest culpability)
  • Manslaughter: Unlawful killing without malice aforethought (lower culpability)

Hierarchy (most to least serious):

  1. First-Degree Murder (premeditated & deliberate, or felony murder)
  2. Second-Degree Murder (all other killings with malice aforethought)
  3. Voluntary Manslaughter (killing in heat of passion with adequate provocation)
  4. Involuntary Manslaughter (killing through criminal negligence)

Malice Aforethought

Definition in murder: Means ANY of the following (despite name "aforethought," doesn't require premeditation):

  1. Intent to kill (express malice)
  2. Intent to cause serious bodily injury
  3. Extreme recklessness/depraved heart (implied malice) - wanton disregard for human life
  4. Commission of dangerous felony (felony murder rule)

Key: "Malice aforethought" is misleading historical term. Doesn't require actual malice (spite), hatred, or premeditation. Just requires one of four mental states above.

Implied Malice (Depraved Heart): Extreme recklessness showing wanton indifference to human life. Examples: Deliberately driving car into crowd; shooting into occupied building; giving lethal drug dose when aware of risk.

Murder vs. Manslaughter Distinction

Murder requires: Malice aforethought (+ additional elements for first-degree).

Voluntary Manslaughter requires: Intent to kill or serious injury (like murder) BUT with provocation that would cause reasonable person to lose self-control, and defendant actually lost self-control in heat of passion without cooling off period. (Mitigates murder to manslaughter.)

Involuntary Manslaughter requires: Killing through criminal negligence (recklessness) without intent to kill.

Significance: Manslaughter sentences vastly shorter than murder sentences. Murder conviction can result in 25-life; manslaughter often 5-15 years.

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Homicide is major exam topic. Expect detailed scenarios testing which homicide category applies.

6. First-Degree Murder (Premeditation & Deliberation)

Definition

First-Degree Murder = Unlawful killing with malice aforethought + (Premeditation AND Deliberation) OR Felony Murder Rule

Two paths: (1) Premeditated & deliberate killing, or (2) Killing during inherently dangerous felony.

Path 1: Premeditation & Deliberation

Requirements: Malice aforethought + Premeditation AND Deliberation

PREMEDITATION: Cool and deliberate thought beforehand; conscious decision to kill formed before the act.

  • Requires: Prior thought and reflection about killing
  • No specific time required (can be instantaneous in some jurisdictions, though "cool reflection" suggests more than momentary)
  • Must be conscious thought to form intent to kill
  • Examples: D goes home, gets gun, returns to kill V = premeditated (time for reflection)
  • Examples: D picks up gun on impulse and shoots V = arguably not premeditated (no prior thought)

DELIBERATION: Cool mind capable of forming intent; weighing decision to kill.

  • Requires: Conscious decision and weighing of alternatives
  • Must be "cool deliberation" not "heat of passion"
  • Examples: D thinks "I could kill him or just injure him" and chooses to kill = deliberate
  • Examples: D kills in blind rage without thinking = not deliberate

Both Required: Premeditation AND Deliberation both necessary. Cannot have one without other for first-degree murder via this path.

Evidence of Premeditation & Deliberation

Courts look to:

  • Motive (why would D want to kill?)
  • Manner of killing (was it brutal/calculated suggesting planning?)
  • Weapons used (did D bring weapon suggesting planning?)
  • Prior threats or statements (did D express intent beforehand?)
  • Conduct after killing (did D try to cover up suggesting consciousness of guilt?)
  • Relationship between parties (was there history of conflict?)
  • Time between decision and act (was there time for reflection?)

Note: These are circumstantial evidence categories. Jury infers premeditation from circumstances.

Key Distinction: Premeditation vs. Deliberation

Premeditation: Temporal element - prior thought, reflection, time to kill

Deliberation: Mental element - cool deliberate weighing of options

Both must exist: D could premeditate (think about it for days) but act in heat of passion when confronting victim (no deliberation) = not first-degree murder

Alternatively: D could form intent in heat of moment (no premeditation) but still deliberate once emotion settles = depends on jurisdiction whether instant deliberation possible.

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Premeditation and deliberation heavily tested. Expect scenarios where these elements are disputed.

Hypothetical: Rick and Morty - Premeditation vs. Deliberation

Rick discovers that his nemesis, Evil Morty, has been secretly sabotaging his inventions for months. Rick is furious. He goes to his garage, spends 3 hours building a specialized gun that fires explosive rounds. He then goes to Evil Morty's location and shoots him with the gun. The explosion kills Evil Morty instantly. Rick is charged with first-degree murder.

ANALYSIS - Malice Aforethought: Does Rick have malice aforethought? YES. Rick clearly intends to kill Evil Morty (malice element satisfied).

ANALYSIS - Premeditation: Did Rick premeditate (form conscious intent to kill beforehand)?

  • Time for reflection: Rick spent 3 hours building specialized gun, so YES, had time to think about it
  • Prior thought: When Rick discovered sabotage, he then went to garage and deliberately constructed weapon designed to kill, so YES, formed intent beforehand
  • Conscious formation: Rick's act of building specialized killing weapon shows conscious decision to kill, not impulse
  • Conclusion: PREMEDITATION EXISTS

ANALYSIS - Deliberation: Did Rick deliberate (cool, conscious weighing of options)?

  • Cool mind? Rick spent 3 hours in garage building weapon. This suggests he cooled off and deliberately planned, not acting in blind rage
  • Weighing alternatives? Rick could have: (a) confronted Evil Morty and demanded explanation, (b) disabled his weapons, (c) reported him, (d) killed him. Rick chose killing, suggesting deliberate weighing
  • Heat of passion? Rick was angry initially (discovering sabotage) but had 3 hours to cool off. By time he built weapon, he was in calm, deliberative state, not heat of passion
  • Conclusion: DELIBERATION EXISTS

OUTCOME: Both premeditation AND deliberation satisfied. Rick guilty of first-degree murder (assuming no other defenses).

Contrast Scenario: Suppose Rick discovered sabotage, was in garage for unrelated reason, immediately saw Evil Morty, grabbed closest weapon (wrench), and beat him to death in seconds without any prior intent to kill. Here: Premeditation FAILS (no prior thought/intent); only instantaneous decision to kill. Depending on jurisdiction, deliberation might also fail (heat of passion). Result: Second-degree murder at most (killed with malice/intent but without premeditation/deliberation), or potentially voluntary manslaughter if provocation was adequate.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Difference between premeditation (temporal, prior thought) and deliberation (mental, cool reflection), (2) Both must exist for first-degree, (3) Time spent before act evidences premeditation, (4) Lack of heat of passion evidences deliberation, (5) Jury infers these from circumstantial evidence.

7. Second-Degree Murder (Implied Malice/Depraved Heart)

Definition

Second-Degree Murder = Unlawful killing with malice aforethought (all cases not meeting first-degree requirements)

Catches all murders that don't meet premeditation/deliberation requirement or felony murder rule. Includes implied malice murders.

Implied Malice (Depraved Heart)

Definition: Extreme recklessness showing wanton indifference to human life. Acting with knowledge that conduct creates substantial risk of death, and act shows such extreme recklessness as to imply intent to kill.

Key distinction from recklessness: Implied malice in murder context requires EXTREME recklessness, not ordinary recklessness. Higher threshold than simple reckless endangerment.

Examples:

  • D deliberately drives car into crowd = implied malice
  • D shoots gun into occupied building = implied malice
  • D drives 80 mph through school yard full of children = implied malice (if aware of children)
  • D gives lethal drug dose when aware of risk = implied malice if knew risk was extreme
  • D leaves victim bleeding in desert to die slowly = implied malice (wanton indifference)

Mental State: Does NOT require intent to kill. Only requires extreme recklessness/wanton indifference. Different from express malice (which requires intent to kill).

Second-Degree Murder Categories

Can be committed in multiple ways:

  1. Intent to kill: D intends to kill but doesn't premeditate/deliberate (instantaneous decision) = second-degree
  2. Intent to cause serious bodily injury: D intends to cause serious injury (not kill) but death results = second-degree (malice element satisfied)
  3. Implied malice (extreme recklessness): D acts with extreme recklessness/depraved heart without any intent to kill = second-degree
  4. Felony murder (some jurisdictions): Death during inherently dangerous felony (if not first-degree qualifying felony)

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Implied malice heavily tested, especially distinguishing from voluntary manslaughter (heat of passion).

Hypothetical: Family Guy - Implied Malice vs. Voluntary Manslaughter

Peter Griffin learns that Lois has been texting her ex-boyfriend. Peter is enraged and hurt. He goes to a bar and gets drunk. Later that night, Peter drives home at 70 mph in a 25 mph residential street (after drinking heavily), runs a red light, and hits Meg, his daughter, who was walking home from her friend's house. Meg dies from her injuries. Peter is charged with murder (implied malice).

ANALYSIS - Murder vs. Manslaughter Issue: Is this murder (implied malice) or manslaughter?

Argument for Second-Degree Murder (Implied Malice):

  • Extreme Recklessness: Peter drove 70 in 25 mph zone + ran red light + was drunk = extreme recklessness exceeding mere recklessness
  • Wanton Indifference to Human Life: Peter knew residential street = children walking around. Driving drunk at triple the speed limit in residential area shows indifference to specific risk (hitting children)
  • No Need for Intent to Kill: Peter didn't intend to kill Meg. But extreme recklessness = implied malice for murder purposes
  • Degree of Risk: Risk was extremely high/substantial that someone would be killed; Peter acted anyway
  • Conclusion: SECOND-DEGREE MURDER plausible

Counterargument for Manslaughter (Criminal Negligence):

  • Criminal Negligence Standard: Driving drunk + speeding in residential area = recklessness (conscious awareness of risk + disregard), not mere negligence
  • But Recklessness might not be "Extreme": Peter was drunk; was he consciously aware he'd hit someone? Or was he negligently unaware due to intoxication?
  • Heat of Passion Argument: Peter was emotionally distraught (Lois/ex-boyfriend). Could argue recklessness was partly emotional disturbance, not pure "depraved heart"
  • If recklessness deemed not "extreme" enough: Would be involuntary manslaughter (killing by recklessness without malice)

Likely Outcome: Prosecution would charge second-degree murder (implied malice). Jury might find either: (1) Guilty of second-degree murder (extreme recklessness), or (2) Guilty of involuntary manslaughter (recklessness but not extreme enough for malice), depending on jury's view of how extreme/wanton the conduct was. Key question: Did Peter's recklessness rise to level of "wanton indifference to human life" (murder), or was it "mere" recklessness (manslaughter)?

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Difference between implied malice (extreme recklessness = murder) and regular recklessness (= manslaughter), (2) Factors showing "extreme" or "wanton" indifference: speed + intoxication + residential area + high risk, (3) Intent to kill NOT required for implied malice murder, (4) Jury's judgment call on where line is between recklessness and extreme recklessness.

8. Voluntary Manslaughter (Heat of Passion, Adequate Provocation)

Definition

Voluntary Manslaughter = Unlawful killing with intent to kill or intent to cause serious bodily injury, BUT committed in heat of passion provoked by adequate provocation, without cooling-off period

Mitigates murder to manslaughter. Requires defendant had malice (intent to kill/injure) but emotional state was provoked.

Elements

1. Adequate Provocation (Objective Test - What would provoke reasonable person?)

Traditional adequate provocations:

  • Discovery of spousal infidelity: Spouse in bed with another person = adequate provocation
  • Assault (significant): Serious physical attack on defendant or close family member
  • Illegal arrest or serious mistreatment: Being arrested illegally or severely beaten by police
  • Mutual combat/fighting: Getting into fight where both parties engaged

NOT traditionally adequate: Words alone, insults, minor bumps, discovery of non-spousal infidelity (your girlfriend cheating, but not spouse)

Modern trend: Many jurisdictions broadening what counts as adequate provocation beyond traditional categories, looking at whether reasonable person would lose control.

Elements (Continued)

2. Heat of Passion (Subjective Test - Did defendant actually lose control?)

  • Defendant must have actually lost self-control in response to provocation
  • Not premeditated or deliberate (distinguishes from first-degree murder)
  • Emotional state prevents cool deliberation
  • Killing must be immediate response to provocation

3. No Cooling-Off Period

  • Defendant must kill while still in heat of passion
  • If sufficient time passes for reasonable person to cool off, heat of passion defense fails
  • Examples: If D is provoked, leaves scene, drives home, then returns to kill V = cooling-off period occurred
  • Time factor: Courts consider whether sufficient time passed for reasonable person to regain control

4. Causal Connection

  • Provocation must cause the heat of passion (provocation didn't lead to other emotional state)
  • Heat of passion must lead to killing (not to assault that coincidentally caused death)

Key Distinctions

Voluntary Manslaughter vs. Second-Degree Murder (Implied Malice): Both involve intent to kill, but voluntary manslaughter has provocation/emotional context mitigating the crime, while implied malice has wanton indifference context. Provocation = manslaughter; wanton indifference = murder.

Voluntary Manslaughter vs. Involuntary Manslaughter: Voluntary requires intent to kill (just with provocation mitigating it); involuntary is killing by recklessness/negligence without intent to kill.

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Heat of passion and adequate provocation heavily tested. Expect scenarios where provocation/cooling-off periods disputed.

Hypothetical: Family Guy - Heat of Passion and Cooling-Off Period

Peter Griffin comes home unexpectedly from work and finds Lois in bed with Quagmire (his best friend). Peter is instantly enraged, grabbing a lamp from the bedroom nightstand and beating Quagmire to death with it. Quagmire dies from his injuries. Peter is charged with first-degree murder. Peter's defense is voluntary manslaughter (heat of passion).

ANALYSIS - Malice Aforethought: Does Peter have malice? YES. Peter intends to kill Quagmire (shown by grabbing lamp and beating him to death). So Peter satisfies malice element required for murder.

ANALYSIS - Adequate Provocation: Is discovery of spousal infidelity adequate provocation? YES. Traditional adequate provocation category. Reasonable person would lose self-control discovering spouse in bed with another. Provocation element satisfied.

ANALYSIS - Heat of Passion: Did Peter actually lose self-control in response? YES. Peter didn't calmly confront Quagmire; he immediately grabbed lamp and struck repeatedly. This is not deliberate, premeditated act but immediate emotional response showing loss of control. Heat of passion element satisfied.

ANALYSIS - Cooling-Off Period: Did sufficient time pass for Peter to cool off? NO. Peter walked in, saw infidelity, immediately attacked with nearby lamp. No time passed between provocation and killing. Cooling-off element satisfied (there was no cooling-off period).

OUTCOME: First-degree murder charge fails because: (1) No premeditation (instant decision), (2) No deliberation (heat of passion, not cool). Would be VOLUNTARY MANSLAUGHTER because all elements satisfied: malice (intent to kill) + adequate provocation + heat of passion + no cooling-off period.

Contrast Scenario: Suppose Peter discovered infidelity, left the bedroom in anger, went downstairs, sat in living room for 2 hours seething, then went back upstairs, armed himself with a knife (showing planning), and killed Quagmire. Here: Cooling-off period occurred (2 hours = ample time for reasonable person to cool off). Retrieving weapon = new formation of intent. This becomes FIRST-DEGREE MURDER (premeditation + deliberation). Heat of passion defense fails because cooling-off period occurred.

Another Contrast: Suppose Peter killed Quagmire, but did so by carefully poisoning his drink (not in moment of discovery, but later, with planning). This would be PREMEDITATED FIRST-DEGREE MURDER, not voluntary manslaughter, because no heat of passion (too much time passed and planning occurred).

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Adequate provocation categories (spousal infidelity classic case), (2) Difference between malice element (which voluntary manslaughter HAS) and heat of passion mitigating factor, (3) Cooling-off period and how time matters, (4) No premeditation/deliberation in heat of passion (distinguishes from first-degree), (5) Any intervening time (leaving scene, waiting, planning) breaks heat of passion.

9. Involuntary Manslaughter (Criminal Negligence)

Definition

Involuntary Manslaughter = Unlawful killing without intent to kill or intent to cause serious bodily injury, committed through recklessness or criminal negligence

Lowest category of homicide. Requires no malice aforethought, only recklessness or criminal negligence.

Two Paths to Involuntary Manslaughter

Path 1: Recklessness

  • Defendant is aware of substantial risk of death
  • Defendant consciously disregards that risk
  • Conduct grossly deviates from ordinary person's conduct
  • Death results from that reckless conduct
  • Example: D recklessly drives 80 mph through school zone; hits child; child dies
  • Example: D fires gun in direction where people might be; person dies

Path 2: Criminal Negligence

  • Defendant is UNAWARE of substantial risk (unlike recklessness)
  • Reasonable person would have been aware of substantial risk
  • Conduct grossly deviates from ordinary person's conduct
  • Death results from that negligent conduct
  • Example: D doesn't see pedestrian and hits them with car; pedestrian dies (D wasn't aware of risk, but should have been)
  • Example: D drives with defective brakes; doesn't know they're defective; hits someone; they die

Key Distinctions

Involuntary Manslaughter vs. Murder: Murder requires malice (intent to kill, intent to cause serious injury, or extreme recklessness). Involuntary manslaughter requires only ordinary recklessness or negligence, not extreme/wanton recklessness.

Recklessness vs. Criminal Negligence: Recklessness requires consciousness of risk; criminal negligence doesn't. Both require gross deviation from ordinary conduct.

Involuntary Manslaughter vs. Voluntary Manslaughter: Involuntary has no intent to kill; voluntary has intent to kill (but is mitigated by provocation).

Exam Likelihood: HIGH. Expect scenarios involving reckless or negligent conduct causing death. Testing line between involuntary manslaughter and second-degree murder (implied malice).

Hypothetical: Ted (Mark Wahlberg) - Criminal Negligence and Recklessness

Ted is driving late at night on a residential street. Ted is exhausted (hasn't slept in 30 hours) but decides to drive home anyway. Ted falls asleep at the wheel, crosses the center line, and crashes into an oncoming car driven by John's girlfriend. She dies from her injuries. Ted had no awareness of the risk of falling asleep (because Ted was extremely fatigued and losing judgment), but a reasonable person would have known they should pull over. Ted is charged with involuntary manslaughter.

ANALYSIS - Lack of Intent to Kill: Ted has no intent to kill John's girlfriend. This is not murder (no malice). Question is whether involuntary manslaughter (criminal negligence) or voluntary manslaughter (but heat of passion doesn't apply here).

ANALYSIS - Criminal Negligence Path:

  • Was Ted aware of risk? NO. Ted was so exhausted he was losing consciousness. Ted may have been unaware of risk (though objective test applies)
  • Should reasonable person have been aware? YES. Reasonable person would know driving 30 hours without sleep is dangerous and would pull over or get ride
  • Gross deviation from ordinary conduct? YES. Ordinary careful driver would not drive while dangerously fatigued
  • Causation? YES. Ted's negligent driving (falling asleep) caused the accident and death
  • Conclusion: INVOLUNTARY MANSLAUGHTER via criminal negligence = GUILTY

ANALYSIS - Could be recklessness instead:

  • If Ted WAS aware of risk (knew he was exhausted, knew it was dangerous) but consciously disregarded it (drove anyway without stopping) = recklessness
  • If recklessness proven, still involuntary manslaughter, same result

Contrast Scenario for Second-Degree Murder: Suppose Ted was ALSO driving 80 mph in a 30 mph residential zone, AND Ted had consumed alcohol (though not necessarily over legal limit), AND Ted was racing to beat a red light. Now conduct shows extreme indifference to human life (not just negligence). Multiple factors (speed + substance + recklessness) combine to show wanton indifference. Could be argued as second-degree murder (implied malice) rather than involuntary manslaughter. Jury would decide if conduct was "extreme recklessness" (murder) or "ordinary recklessness" (manslaughter).

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Criminal negligence vs. recklessness (awareness vs. no awareness of risk), (2) Objective standard for criminal negligence (would reasonable person know?), (3) Involuntary manslaughter as lowest homicide category, (4) Line between involuntary manslaughter and second-degree murder (ordinary vs. extreme recklessness), (5) Causation (negligent driving caused death).

10. Felony Murder Rule (Inherently Dangerous Felony, Merger Doctrine, Agency vs. Proximate Cause)

Definition

Felony Murder Rule: A death occurring during commission of inherently dangerous felony is automatically murder, even if defendant didn't intend to kill or was unaware of risk of death

Harsh rule: Malice is implied by felony commission; no need to prove intent to kill or even recklessness toward death.

Elements

1. Commission of Inherently Dangerous Felony

Not all felonies qualify. Only felonies that are inherently dangerous (create substantial risk of death):

  • Qualifying felonies: Robbery, burglary, rape, arson, kidnapping
  • Non-qualifying example: Tax evasion, embezzlement, forgery (don't inherently create risk of death)
  • Uncertain cases: Drug dealing, driving with suspended license (courts disagree)

2. Death Occurs During Commission of Felony

  • Felony must be ongoing when death occurs
  • Death during perpetration counts
  • Death during flight from felony may or may not count (depends on jurisdiction and whether flight is part of "commission")

3. Causation (Proximate or Agency Test)

  • Defendant's conduct (felony) must have caused the death
  • Courts split on test: Proximate cause test or agency test

Two Main Causation Tests for Felony Murder

Proximate Cause Test: Death must be foreseeable result of felony. If victim or bystander dies in foreseeable way, felony murder applies even if caused by someone else.

  • Example: D robs store; clerk tries to stop D; owner shoots clerk (accidentally trying to hit D). D liable for murder even though owner pulled trigger.
  • Rationale: Death was foreseeable consequence of robbery

Agency Test (Narrower): Death must be caused by act of defendant or co-felon; not by independent act of victim or third party.

  • Example: D robs store; clerk tries to stop D; owner shoots clerk. Under agency test, D NOT liable because owner (not D or co-felon) caused death.
  • Rationale: Don't hold D liable for acts of people not in conspiracy with D

Significance: Proximate cause test is broader, resulting in more felony murder convictions.

Merger Doctrine

Definition: Crime that forms basis of felony murder cannot also be the underlying felony (to prevent abuse).

Example (merger problem): D assaults V; V dies from injuries. Can't prosecute for felony murder based on assault resulting in death (assault is the same crime as the result), or would make all assaults resulting in death into murders.

Rule: Assault doesn't qualify as predicate felony for felony murder (in most jurisdictions) because assault is inherently about causing injury; makes no sense to say "inherently dangerous felony" when felony itself is dangerous act toward that victim.

Rationale: Prevent punishing D twice for same conduct. If D commits assault that kills V, that's assault or manslaughter charge, not assault + felony murder.

Exam Likelihood: VERY HIGH. Felony murder heavily tested. Expect scenarios with deaths during robberies/burglaries and questions about causation/foreseeability.

Hypothetical: Ted (Mark Wahlberg) - Felony Murder and Proximate Cause

Ted and his friend decide to rob a convenience store. They enter with no weapons but demand money. The store clerk (who is a large, brave person) refuses to give them money and physically tries to stop Ted from leaving with the register. A customer (who is off-duty police officer) is in the store. The customer tries to apprehend Ted. In the chaos, the customer's gun falls out of holster onto the floor. The clerk grabs the gun and, in the struggle, accidentally shoots the customer in the abdomen. The customer dies from the gunshot wound. Ted is charged with felony murder. Ted's defense: "I didn't kill anyone; the clerk did."

ANALYSIS - Felony Murder Elements:

1. Inherently Dangerous Felony? YES. Robbery is classic qualifying felony for felony murder rule (creates substantial risk of harm).

2. Death during commission? YES. Death (customer) occurred during ongoing robbery. The chaos and struggle happened while robbery was in progress.

3. Causation - Key Issue:

Under Proximate Cause Test:

  • Was death a foreseeable result of the robbery? YES. When D commits robbery, it's foreseeable that victim might resist, store employee might resist, bystanders might intervene, struggle might occur, and injury/death might result from struggle.
  • Customer's death was direct, foreseeable consequence of creating dangerous situation through robbery
  • Doesn't matter that clerk (not Ted) pulled trigger; death was foreseeable result of Ted's felony
  • Conclusion under proximate cause test: TED IS GUILTY OF FELONY MURDER

Under Agency Test (Narrower):

  • Did Ted or a co-felon cause the death? NO. The clerk, not Ted or his co-felon friend, shot the customer.
  • The clerk is not a conspirator or co-felon with Ted; clerk is a third party (victim's employee)
  • Death was caused by independent act of clerk (grabbing gun and shooting), not by Ted's conduct
  • Conclusion under agency test: TED IS NOT GUILTY OF FELONY MURDER (would be guilty of robbery only)

Likely Outcome: Depends on jurisdiction. Proximate cause jurisdictions (majority) = TED GUILTY. Agency test jurisdictions (minority) = TED NOT GUILTY of felony murder (guilty of robbery).

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Felony murder rule and inherently dangerous felony requirement, (2) Proximate cause vs. agency test and major difference in outcomes, (3) Whether deaths caused by third parties (not defendant) qualify for felony murder (depends on test), (4) Foreseeability of resistance/struggle during robbery, (5) Merger doctrine doesn't apply here (robbery and murder are separate crimes).

11. Insanity Defense (M'Naghten, Irresistible Impulse, Durham, ALI/MPC)

Doctrine Overview

Insanity Defense: Even if defendant committed actus reus with mens rea, defendant may not be held criminally responsible if defendant was insane at time of crime.

Key: Insanity is excuse defense (says "I did the act, but I shouldn't be punished because I was insane"), not denial of elements.

Burden: Varies by jurisdiction. Some place burden on defendant (preponderance); others require prosecution to prove sanity (beyond reasonable doubt).

M'Naghten Rule (Most Common)

Definition: Defendant is insane if, at time of crime, due to mental illness, defendant didn't know: (1) the nature of the act, OR (2) the wrongfulness of the act.

Two prongs (either satisfied = insanity):

  • Didn't know nature of act: Defendant didn't understand what they were doing. Example: D thinks gun isn't real; shoots victim thinking it's prop gun.
  • Didn't know wrongfulness: Defendant didn't know act was illegal/wrong. Example: D has delusion that God commanded killing; D thought obedience to God was right.

Cognitive Test: M'Naghten focuses on cognitive (thinking) ability, not emotional ability. Doesn't excuse those who know right from wrong but can't control themselves.

Limitation: Can't use insanity if defendant KNEW the wrongfulness but did it anyway. Only lack of knowledge excuses.

Examples:

  • D with schizophrenia believes victim is alien; kills "alien" thinking it's self-defense = insane (doesn't know act's nature due to delusion)
  • D kills V at God's command; D genuinely believes killing is right = insane (doesn't know wrongfulness due to delusion)
  • D kills to get money (knows it's wrong but does it anyway) = NOT insane (knew wrongfulness)
  • D kills in uncontrollable rage (knows it's wrong but can't stop self) = NOT insane under M'Naghten (knew wrongfulness)

Irresistible Impulse Test

Definition: Insanity defense if mental illness prevented defendant from resisting impulse to commit crime, even if defendant knew act was wrong.

Addresses M'Naghten limitation: M'Naghten focuses on knowledge; irresistible impulse focuses on control/volition.

Requirements:

  • Defendant had mental illness
  • Mental illness created impulse to commit crime
  • Defendant lacked capacity to control/resist impulse
  • Impulse was sudden (though some say not strictly required)

Example: D has OCD with intrusive thoughts commanding harm to others; despite knowing killing is wrong, D's brain chemistry makes impulse irresistible; D kills.

Distinction from M'Naghten: D can know act is wrong (satisfies M'Naghten) but still be insane under irresistible impulse if can't control behavior.

Criticism: How to prove impulse was "irresistible"? Hard to distinguish from "I just did it" or "I have poor self-control."

Durham Rule (Product Test) - Rare

Definition: Insanity if defendant's criminal conduct was product of mental illness.

Broader than M'Naghten: Doesn't require cognitive/knowledge test; just requires causal link between mental illness and crime.

Example: D has depression; depression caused D to commit suicide; if crime occurred during suicide attempt (e.g., killing self and family in murder-suicide), could be insane under Durham (crime is product of illness).

Criticism: Too broad; almost any crime could be product of mental illness. Mostly abandoned.

Used in: Very few jurisdictions now (maybe New Hampshire).

ALI/MPC Test (Cognitive + Volitional)

Definition: Insanity if mental illness prevented defendant from either: (1) appreciating the criminality of conduct (cognitive), OR (2) conforming conduct to law (volitional).

Two prongs:

  • Appreciate criminality: Similar to M'Naghten (cognitive test) but uses "appreciate" (understanding consequences) not just "know" (intellectual knowing). Broader than M'Naghten.
  • Conform conduct to law: Can't control behavior even though knows it's wrong (like irresistible impulse but less demanding - doesn't require "sudden" impulse)

Most flexible test: Combines cognitive and volitional prongs; allows for impulse control issues.

Example: D has schizophrenia causing both (1) delusions that victim deserves death, AND (2) inability to control violent impulses. Under ALI, D could be insane on either prong.

Used in: Many federal courts and progressive states.

Key Case Law Concept

Different jurisdictions use different tests, so answer depends on which test applies. On exam, if jurisdiction not specified, might need to analyze under multiple tests or state which test you're assuming.

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM-HIGH. Insanity tested, but often only when crime is bizarre or defendant's mental state is prominent. Distinguishing insanity from lack of mens rea is common issue.

Hypothetical: Rick and Morty - Insanity and Knowledge of Wrongfulness

Rick is developing a severe neurological illness that causes deteriorating mental function. Rick begins experiencing paranoid delusions that the government is tracking him through implanted microchips. Rick becomes convinced that Morty is actually a government agent replacing his real grandson. Rick forms the delusional belief that killing "government agent Morty" is necessary self-defense against surveillance network. Rick kills "Morty" by poisoning his food. Later, doctors diagnose Rick with a psychotic disorder. Rick is charged with murder. Rick's defense: Insanity.

ANALYSIS - M'Naghten Test (Knowledge-Based):

Did Rick know nature of the act? Rick knew he was poisoning and killing someone. Rick understood the physical act. So YES, Rick knew nature of act.

Did Rick know wrongfulness of the act? KEY QUESTION. Rick's delusion: He believed he was defending himself against government surveillance. Under his delusional system, killing government agent is JUSTIFIED SELF-DEFENSE (rightful).

  • If we evaluate wrongfulness objectively (actual law): Poisoning is wrong; you can't kill government agents in "self-defense" from surveillance
  • If we evaluate wrongfulness from Rick's delusional perspective: Rick believed killing was rightful self-defense against legitimate threat
  • M'Naghten standard: "Didn't know wrongfulness" typically means the ACTOR's perspective due to mental illness, not objective law
  • So: Rick, due to delusion, didn't know act was wrongful (thought it was justified); Rick is INSANE under M'Naghten

ANALYSIS - Irresistible Impulse Test: Did Rick's mental illness prevent him from resisting impulse to poison? Rick planned poisoning (not sudden impulse). Rick had time to reflect. Seems like impulse WAS resistible if Rick could deliberate about it. So probably NOT insane under irresistible impulse test alone. But combined with M'Naghten, insane on M'Naghten grounds is sufficient.

ANALYSIS - ALI/MPC Test:

  • Appreciate criminality: Rick's delusion prevented appreciation of wrongfulness (thought it was justified defense). INSANE.
  • Conform conduct: Rick couldn't conform even if he understood law (delusional belief overpowers voluntary control). INSANE.

Contrast - Malingering/Fake Insanity: Suppose Rick faked the delusions to get out of trouble (actually knew what he was doing, knew it was wrong, but claimed insanity). M'Naghten would fail (Rick actually knew wrongfulness). Courts are skeptical of last-minute insanity claims and require expert testimony about mental illness.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) M'Naghten knowledge test and how it applies to delusional defendants, (2) Whether defendant evaluates wrongfulness objectively or from own perspective (usually own perspective when mental illness involved), (3) Difference between knowing act is illegal and knowing it's morally wrong (delusion affects both), (4) Other insanity tests and how they might apply, (5) Burden of proof and how defendant raises insanity defense.

12. Sexual Assault & Consent

Doctrine Overview

Sexual Assault (Rape): Non-consensual sexual penetration (or sexual contact, depending on statute). Requires: (1) sexual activity, (2) lack of consent, (3) sometimes force/threat.

Evolution: Traditional law focused on force; modern law focuses on lack of consent (better reflects harm).

Consent Definition: Affirmative, voluntary agreement to sexual activity. Must be informed, given freely (without coercion), and capable of being revoked.

Elements of Sexual Assault

1. Sexual Activity/Conduct

  • Penetration of sexual organ with another sexual organ or object
  • Sexual contact (touching sexual organ, breast, buttocks)
  • Degree of penetration varies by jurisdiction and statute
  • Oral sex typically qualifies as rape

2. Lack of Consent

  • Consent must be affirmative (saying yes, not just not saying no)
  • Consent must be voluntary (not coerced, threatened, or compelled)
  • Consent must be informed (understanding what's happening)
  • Can be withdrawn at any time during activity

3. Force/Coercion/Threat (depending on jurisdiction)

  • Traditional requirement: Must use force (physical force, threat of force)
  • Modern requirement: Some jurisdictions only require lack of consent (no force threshold needed)
  • Statutory rape: No force required; consent impossible due to age (victim under age of consent)

Consent Defenses and Issues

Incapacity to Consent (No Consent Possible):

  • Age: Person below age of consent cannot legally consent, regardless of actual willingness. Statutory rape.
  • Intoxication: Person too intoxicated to consent (unconscious, severely impaired). Consent impossible if victim can't understand what's happening.
  • Mental incapacity: Mental illness, developmental disability preventing understanding of consequences.
  • Sleep/unconsciousness: Cannot consent while asleep or unconscious.
  • Relationship power imbalances: Some jurisdictions recognize lack of consent in severely unequal power relationships (professor-student, therapist-patient, coach-athlete).

Affirmative Consent Model (Modern): "Yes means yes" - requires affirmative expression of consent, not just absence of "no." Shifting burden to defendant to ensure consent.

Lack of Resistance Model (Traditional, less common now): "No means no" - requires victim to say no or resist; absence of resistance = consent. Criticized as unfair to victims who freeze or can't physically resist.

Mens Rea for Sexual Assault

Varies by jurisdiction:

  • Knowing lack of consent: Defendant must know victim didn't consent (subjective standard)
  • Recklessness regarding consent: Defendant aware of risk victim doesn't consent but disregards it
  • Negligence regarding consent: Defendant should have known victim didn't consent (objective standard - defendant failed to check/ask)
  • Strict liability: Some jurisdictions (rare) make rape strict liability as to consent element (no mens rea required regarding consent)

Defendant's honest belief in consent: Depends on jurisdiction. Some accept reasonable belief in consent as defense; others require defendant to act reasonably in determining consent.

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM-HIGH. Sexual assault scenarios commonly tested, especially consent and intoxication issues. Merger doctrine may also be tested (assault + rape).

Hypothetical: South Park - Consent and Intoxication

Kyle and Stan are college roommates. One night, they go to a party where both drink heavily. Kyle becomes extremely drunk (slurring speech, unsteady, appears impaired). Stan is also drunk but less so. Late that night, Stan helps drunk Kyle back to their dorm room. Stan removes Kyle's clothes and initiates sexual contact with Kyle while Kyle is barely conscious. Kyle doesn't explicitly say "no," but is too intoxicated to actively participate or express affirmative consent. The next morning, Kyle has no clear memory of what happened. Kyle later reports this to campus authorities. Stan is charged with sexual assault/rape. Stan's defense: "Kyle never said no. I thought Kyle wanted it."

ANALYSIS - Consent Element:

Can Kyle consent while extremely intoxicated? NO. Kyle was so intoxicated that Kyle couldn't understand what was happening, couldn't communicate consent, and couldn't make affirmative decision about sexual activity. Legal standard for incapacity due to intoxication is whether victim was so impaired as to be unable to:

  • Understand what's happening (Kyle couldn't remember events = couldn't understand)
  • Communicate consent/refusal (Kyle was barely conscious)
  • Make informed decision (Kyle was too intoxicated to process)

Kyle's lack of resistance = consent? Under modern "affirmative consent" standard: NO. Lack of "no" is not same as "yes." Affirmative consent requires actual agreement, not just absence of refusal. Kyle's silence/non-resistance due to intoxication is NOT consent.

Under traditional "no means no" model: Would argue Kyle didn't say "no," so consent might be implied. However, courts applying this test still recognize incapacity exception: Victims too intoxicated to communicate are incapable of consenting, period.

ANALYSIS - Mens Rea Regarding Consent:

Did Stan know Kyle lacked capacity to consent?

  • Kyle was barely conscious, slurring speech, helped to room
  • Reasonable person would perceive Kyle was too intoxicated
  • Stan either knew or should have known Kyle couldn't consent
  • If jurisdiction requires knowing lack of consent: Stan probably knew (saw Kyle's impairment)
  • If jurisdiction requires recklessness: Stan was aware of risk (obvious intoxication) and disregarded it
  • If jurisdiction requires negligence: Stan failed to check if Kyle could consent (should have asked clearly, waited for clarity)

OUTCOME: Stan GUILTY of sexual assault/rape. Kyle's incapacity due to intoxication means no consent was possible. Stan's failure to ensure affirmative consent, combined with clear intoxication indicators, establishes mens rea (knew or should have known of lack of consent). Stan's defense ("Kyle didn't say no") fails because lack of "no" doesn't equal "yes" when victim is incapacitated.

EXAM ISSUE: This tests: (1) Incapacity to consent due to intoxication, (2) Affirmative consent standard (requires yes, not absence of no), (3) Mens rea regarding consent (knowing vs. reckless vs. negligent), (4) Defendant's duty to verify consent before sexual activity, (5) How intoxication affects both parties' ability to consent (interesting if both were equally intoxicated, courts still usually hold more-sober partner responsible for getting affirmative consent), (6) Distinction between statutory consent (too young) and factual consent (too intoxicated).

1. Theories of Property: Sovereignty, Labor, and Discovery Doctrine

Foundational Principles

Property is NOT a right to a thing, but relationships between people with respect to valued resources. Property consists of a bundle of rights: right to exclude, right to use/enjoy, right to transfer, right to security, right to divide, right to devise, right to destroy. Property is a creation of law and government—government allocates, protects, enforces, and can curtail property rights.

Key Justifications:

  • Security: Protection of holdings enables planning
  • Efficiency: Creates incentives for productive use and investment
  • Freedom: Personal autonomy in using one's resources
  • Personhood: Property tied to identity and self-expression
  • Distributive Justice: Determines who gets what resources

Johnson v. McIntosh (1823)

Doctrine: Discovery Doctrine

Facts: Dispute over North American land between European powers and claims derived from Native American titles.

Holding: European discoverers of North American land acquired property rights against other European competitors, subject to Native American "title of occupancy." Federal government has exclusive right to extinguish Indian title.

Key Principles:

  • Discovery doctrine is about relations between competing European powers
  • Native Americans have inferior right; federal government has exclusive right to extinguish Indian title
  • European conquest creates sovereignty and property rights

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Foundational case on property theory. Tests understanding of how law facilitates dispossession and sovereignty claims. May appear in essays asking about justifications for property or historical injustice.

Critical Analysis: Marshall acknowledges the doctrine is controversial but deems courts unable to question the outer boundaries of sovereign. The case represents judicial complicity with colonialism—courts claim to be merely "applying law" when they are actually facilitating dispossession.

Pop Culture Hypothetical: South Park's South Park Property

The town of South Park sits on land originally inhabited by Ute Nation tribes. In the 1800s, the U.S. government granted the land to European settlers under the Discovery Doctrine. Today, the Ute Nation files suit claiming the land was never validly taken and demanding return of the property.

Kyle's Dad (Gerald Broflovski): argues that the government legitimately acquired the land through Discovery Doctrine—European sovereign power gave them rights against other Europeans, and federal government validly extinguished Indian title.

Stan's Dad (Randy Marsh): as a property owner, claims his title is valid and cannot be questioned now, over 150 years later. "We bought in good faith!"

ANALYSIS: Under Johnson v. McIntosh, the court would likely find that the Discovery Doctrine, while harsh, does give federal government authority to extinguish Native American title. However, modern courts might recognize that: (1) discovery doctrine is a legal fiction that facilitated colonialism, (2) Native American claims may survive as historical injustice, and (3) subsequent good-faith purchasers (like Randy) may have acquired valid title through sovereign conveyance, but this doesn't erase the foundational wrong. The case tests whether law can justify historical dispossession and whether courts should question the legitimacy of doctrines that facilitate injustice.

2. Property & Slavery: Property Law's Moral Limits

The Antelope (1825)

Doctrine: Property in Enslaved Persons under Law of Nations

Facts: American privateer captured enslaved Africans from multiple ships (Spanish, Portuguese, American). Case determines which nation can claim them as property.

Holding: Marshall recognizes property in enslaved Africans under "law of nations" (international law). Spanish claims valid (sufficient proof of ownership); enslaved persons returned to Spanish. Portuguese claims insufficient proof; enslaved persons became U.S. property.

Key Reasoning:

  • Slavery originates from accepted international precedent: warfare permits victors to enslave vanquished
  • All civilized nations have acquiesced to this right
  • Christian nations view slavery as morally repugnant BUT international law still requires respecting it
  • Court accepts law of nations over liberty/natural law principles

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of how law can recognize property in humans and how courts use "law of nations" and international comity to justify enforcement of unjust arrangements. May appear in essays on moral limits of property law or institutional competence (whether courts should question legal doctrines).

Critical Analysis—Institutional Competence: Marshall had alternatives: (1) could have invoked U.S. law to prevent slavery, (2) could have revised law of nations interpretation to exclude slavery, (3) could have cited growing anti-slavery precedent. He chose none of these because of: domestic divisiveness over slavery, desire for "comity" with Spain/Portugal, and Marshall's own interest as slaveholder. This represents judicial complicity through passive legalism—the court didn't create slavery but enforced it.

Aftermath: Captives died at rate of 1/3 during detention; Spain sold survivors into slavery; others deported to Liberia.

Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857)

Doctrine: Slavery, Property, and Citizenship

Facts: Enslaved man Dred Scott sues for freedom after living in free states and territories.

Holding: Enslaved persons (and even free African Americans) are not citizens. Property in enslaved persons prevails over federal law restrictions. Court strikes down Missouri Compromise and Northwest Ordinance as unconstitutional because they deprive citizens of property (enslaved persons).

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Extreme example of law protecting property interests over fundamental human rights. Tests understanding of how property law can entrench injustice and how law can be weaponized against human dignity.

Critical Flaw: Taney misrepresents original intent; Constitution had drafts explicitly criticizing slavery.

Significance: Intensifies slavery rather than merely acknowledging it. Goes beyond prior cases to categorically exclude African Americans from citizenship. Inflames North-South tensions; contributes to Civil War. REMEDY: Civil Rights Act of 1866 and 14th Amendment abrogate Dred Scott.

Pop Culture Hypothetical: Ted (the Talking Bear)

In an alternate universe, a wealthy collector claims ownership of Ted, a sentient bear who can talk and perform labor. Ted escaped and is now living as free. The collector sues for "replevin" (recovery of property), arguing that Ted is valuable property and has no legal status as a person. The case reaches court.

The Collector: argues that Ted is his property, validly acquired through purchase. "The law recognizes property in animals; Ted just happens to be more intelligent. But property rights protect my investment."

ANALYSIS: This hypothetical tests the moral limits of property law through the slavery cases. Under The Antelope, courts can recognize property in beings capable of labor. Under Dred Scott, courts can deny citizenship to intelligent beings. However, modern law would reject this: Ted's capacity for rational thought and self-determination would render him a "person" for legal purposes, not property. This shows how property law's moral boundaries shifted. The hypothetical forces students to consider: Can sentience/rationality alone justify property status? What distinguishes property from personhood? Where do we draw the line?

Evolution from McIntosh to Antelope to Dred Scott

Pattern of Legal Complicity:

  • McIntosh: Dispossession through discovery doctrine (Native Americans)
  • Antelope: Acknowledges slavery is abhorrent but enforces it anyway
  • Dred Scott: Not merely enforces slavery but extends it and rejects citizenship for African Americans

Common Thread: All use "positivism" (law is as written) to justify morally repugnant outcomes. All show "might makes right"—those with power dispossess those without. Dispossessed are notably absent from record.

3. Possession: Acquiring Property in Different Objects

General Principle: Context Matters

Possession rules differ significantly by TYPE OF THING. Wild animals, baseballs, and oil/gas have different legal rules. Context determines what counts as "possession" and what elements must be satisfied.

Pierson v. Post (1805) — Wild Animals

Doctrine: Occupancy of Wild Animals

Facts: Post hunts fox with hounds on commons; Pearson shoots and captures it; Post sues for conversion.

Holding: Pearson owns the fox (first actual capture). Post's mere pursuit, however ardent, does not create property right.

Three-Part Occupancy Test (Majority):

  1. Unequivocal Intention: Hunter manifests clear intention to appropriate animal
  2. Deprive Natural Liberty: Animal must be deprived of its natural liberty (not just pursuing it)
  3. Certain Control: Animal must be brought within certain control

Important Distinctions:

  • Physical capture NOT required—mere possession of dead animal counts; mortally wounding counts; can trap it
  • Keeble v. Hickeringill principle: Ducks on someone's property belong to property owner; if you trespass to take them, you commit trespass against property owner, not against the wild animal

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Foundational case on possession doctrine. Tests understanding of bright-line rules vs. flexible standards. Frequently tested on exams with hypos about hunting, fishing, capture of valuable things.

Policy Rationales (Majority):

  • Certainty: Bright-line rule prevents endless litigation about when occupation occurs
  • Peace & Order: Prevents disputes among hunters (though ironic—hunter with weapons might react violently to loss)

Livingston's Dissent—Flexible Standard and Custom:

  • Institutional competence argument: Should defer to custom of hunters and municipalities, not impose one rule
  • Barbarak's test better: Obvious pursuit sufficient (standard, not rule)
  • If large hounds pursuing = hunter's property; if small hounds = captor's property
  • Recognizes context and local knowledge
  • Policy argument: Majority rule discourages fox hunting; under Pearson rule, hunters lose after effort—discourages investment in pursuit

Rules vs. Standards: Pierson = rule (bright-line, mechanical); Livingston = standard (flexible, context-dependent).

Popov v. Hayashi (2002) — Baseballs

Doctrine: Possession and Prepossessory Interest

Facts: Baseball stadium during Bonds home run. Popov reaches up, ball touches his glove. He's tackled by mob; ball rolls free. Hayashi (innocent bystander) picks it up. Popov sues.

Holding: Neither fully possessed the ball. Court splits proceeds 50-50.

Key Rules:

  • Possession rules are CONTEXTUAL to the type of thing
  • Baseball custom requires catching AND holding (higher bar than wild animal rule)
  • Court invokes CUSTOM: in baseball, you must secure it in your possession

Prepossessory Interest (New Doctrine): When actor undertakes significant but incomplete steps to acquire property AND efforts interrupted by wrongful acts of others, actor has "prepossessory interest" (not full possessory right).

Equitable Remedy: Court uses equity (fairness) because neither party has clear legal right. Both have superior claims to the world. SOLUTION: Sell ball and split proceeds.

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Modern application of possession doctrine showing how context and custom matter. Tests understanding that different objects require different possession elements. Shows how courts create new doctrines (prepossessory interest) when traditional rules don't fit.

Distinctions from Pierson:

  • Baseballs vs. wild animals
  • Custom is more developed (baseball rules exist)
  • Different setting (organized sport vs. hunting on commons)
  • Higher bar for possession (must catch AND hold)

Controversy: Equity remedy questionable—court "threw up its hands." 50-50 split may not be fair to Popov (who did significant work). Hayashi was innocent bystander (got lucky). But practical argument: no other way to resolve given facts.

Elliff v. Texon Drilling (1948) — Oil and Gas

Doctrine: Ad Coelum + Rule of Capture with Negligence Limitation

Facts: Oil/gas lease dispute. Driscoll (defendant's lessee) drills on her land, well explodes. Fire reaches Elliff land, drains all their minerals. Suit for damages and property rights.

Holding: Two conflicting rules held together:

  1. Ad Coelum ("to the heavens"): Own everything above and below your land
  2. Rule of Capture: Landowner can extract all oil/gas without liability if it comes from common pool

BUT WITH LIMITATION: Negligent waste or destruction imposes liability. Cannot negligently destroy minerals or allow negligent extraction.

Property Right & Possession:

  • Own minerals beneath land
  • Construct possessory right through oil well
  • Can take as much as you want

Liability:

  • No liability for extractive acts drawing minerals from others' property
  • BUT liable if you act negligently in extraction/destruction
  • Standard: What is "negligent"? (No bright-line rule; depends on context)

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of rule of capture and how it applies differently to different resources. Shows tension between certainty (rule of capture) and fairness (negligence standard).

Policy Tension:

  • Rule of capture incentivizes RAPID extraction
  • If everyone extracts quickly = creates pressure/inefficiency
  • Rapid extraction = greater waste (don't extract all oil efficiently)
  • But negligence standard creates uncertainty—what's negligent?

Tragedy of the Commons: Oil/gas in common pool = race to extract. Everyone extracts maximum speed. Individual benefits from adding extraction. Costs spread among all users. Result: overextraction and waste (like overfishing).

Comparison to Wild Animals: Oil/gas UNLIKE wild animals (not moving until pressure released). But constructive possession recognized before actual capture (like Pierson's pursuit).

Pop Culture Hypothetical: Rick and Morty — The Portal Gun Baseball

Rick builds a portal gun that accidentally zips a rare baseball from 1919 (Babe Ruth autographed) out of a museum. The ball appears in Rick's lab. A neighbor (Neighbor Jerry) sees it through the window, reaches in, grabs it, and runs away. Rick chases Jerry into the yard, where both are tackled by an HOA enforcement officer who picks up the ball.

Rick: "I generated that ball through my technological innovation—it's mine!"

Neighbor Jerry: "I possessed it first when I grabbed it from your lab!"

HOA Officer: "I have it now, so it's mine."

ANALYSIS: This tests multiple possession doctrines:

First issue—Rick's claim: Unlike Pierson's hunter, Rick didn't acquire the ball through traditional capture. He created it through technology, which is not a recognized possession method. Rick's best argument is that the ball appeared in his property (his lab), and he had exclusive control—but the ball's origin in theft (from museum) complicates this. Under ad coelum, Rick owns his lab, so things within his lab space belong to him.

Second issue—Jerry vs. Rick: Jerry's grabbing is trespass into Rick's property (the lab). Even if Jerry acquired possession by touching the ball, he did so wrongfully (trespassing). Under Keeble v. Hickeringill principle, Jerry's trespass gives Rick claims against Jerry, and Jerry gains no valid possessory right.

Third issue—HOA Officer: Officer now has physical possession, but gained it through intervening in dispute between Rick and Jerry. Under Popov, if no one had clear possession before officer grabbed it, officer has strongest claim. But facts suggest Rick had constructive possession (in his lab). Officer's possession is wrongful interference.

Likely outcome: Rick wins. His exclusive control over his lab gives him superior rights. Jerry's trespass defeats any claim. Officer's intervention doesn't create superior right.

Why this matters: Tests understanding that possession is contextual, that location/ownership of underlying property matters, and that trespass affects possession rights.

4. Adverse Possession: Acquiring Title Through Unauthorized Use

Doctrine: Five-Element Test (ALL Required)

Adverse possession allows someone to acquire title to property through unauthorized use for a statutory period (varies: 7-21 years depending on state). All five elements must be proven:

1. ACTUAL POSSESSION

  • Using property as owner would
  • Agricultural land: farming, planting, harvesting
  • Residential: building, living, maintaining
  • Level of use consistent with property type
  • Not just occasional trespassing; regular ownership-like use

2. OPEN AND NOTORIOUS

  • OBJECTIVE STANDARD: Would reasonable owner notice?
  • Not secret or hidden use
  • Visible to "all who look"
  • Test: Would owner making reasonable inspections discover?
  • Doesn't require actual notice to owner
  • Must be visible/apparent from inspection

3. EXCLUSIVE POSSESSION

  • Against the true owner
  • Can have guests, friends, but claimant is main exclusive user
  • Shared possession defeats this element
  • Shows claimant uses it as their own, not someone else's agent

4. CONTINUOUS POSSESSION FOR STATUTORY PERIOD

  • Must meet for statutory period (varies by state: 7-21 years)
  • Continuous ≠ every single day
  • Seasonal use okay if consistent with property type
  • Farming: winter fallow is acceptable
  • TACKING DOCTRINE: Can combine predecessor's period if took property from them
    • Requirements: privity between successive possessors (took from one another)
    • Example: A possesses 10 years, transfers to B, B possesses 5 more = 15 years total
    • Cannot combine with unrelated prior user

5. ADVERSE (WITHOUT PERMISSION)

  • Burden on true owner to show permission given
  • Presumption is adverse/non-permissive
  • Express permission defeats claim
  • Implied permission is rare and burden on defendant to show
  • Two approaches by jurisdiction:
    • Objective: Whether a reasonable owner would view as adverse
    • Subjective: Claimant's state of mind (knowingly trespassing vs. innocent mistake of boundary)
  • Different statutes may vary requirements based on state of mind
  • Color of title: Some jurisdictions reduce statutory period if you have defective deed

OUTCOME if all 5 met: Title transfers to adverse possessor. Adverse possessor can receive deed and transfer to others.

Brown v. Gobble — Tacking Doctrine

Facts: Prior owner possessed land for 10 years, then transferred to current claimant. Current claimant possessed for 5 more years.

Holding: 10 + 5 = 15 years total. Meets statutory period through tacking.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of tacking doctrine and how prior possessors' periods can be combined.

Payne v. Sexton — Color of Title

Facts: Defective deed (doesn't satisfy statute of frauds) but claimant uses land.

Holding: Defective deed still allows adverse possession. Shows intent to claim property; can reduce statutory period in some jurisdictions.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that "color of title" (document showing intent to claim) can help adverse possessor, even if document is defective.

Nome 2000 v. Fagerstrom — Scope of Adverse Possession

Facts: Squatters only occupied portion of large tract.

Holding: Squatters only acquire the land they actually possessed. Cannot claim entire tract if only occupied portion.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that adverse possession is limited to land actually possessed, not larger parcels.

Policy Rationales for Adverse Possession

  • Marketability: Removes clouds on title; encourages land development
  • Efficiency: Land stays in productive use; encourages investment
  • Fairness/Repose: After long period, true owner loses right (can't sleep on rights)
  • Stability: Creates certainty for adverse possessors who've invested
  • Punishment: Inattentive true owners lose property
  • Prevents waste: Prevents situations where land lies unused while title is in someone disinterested

Criticisms: Can seem harsh—true owner loses property through inattention. But property law requires owners to pay attention and keep title current. Encourages productive use over idle holdings. Statute of limitations principle (repose) applies throughout law.

Pop Culture Hypothetical: Family Guy — The Griffins' Vacation Home

Peter Griffin owns a small vacation property (inherited from great-grandfather) in rural Pennsylvania. He's never been there. In 1995, Glenn Quagmire (his neighbor) starts using the property: plants crops, builds a shed, maintains the fence, lives there seasonally with a girlfriend. In 2012 (17 years later), Peter discovers the property through property tax research and demands Quagmire leave. Quagmire sues for adverse possession, claiming title.

Peter (True Owner): "I inherited that property; it's mine! Quagmire is a trespasser!"

Quagmire (Adverse Possessor): "I've lived here openly for 17 years, improved it, and Peter didn't know or care!"

ANALYSIS OF FIVE ELEMENTS:

1. Actual Possession? YES. Quagmire used property as owner would: farming, building shed, maintaining fence, living there. This is true ownership-level use of agricultural property.

2. Open and Notorious? YES. Quagmire's presence is visible to reasonable owner making inspections. Crops growing, shed built, fence maintained, vehicle present—all signs of occupancy. This would be obvious to anyone who visited or hired someone to check on the property.

3. Exclusive Possession? LIKELY YES. Quagmire uses property as sole occupant. He doesn't share with Peter or other co-tenants. Uses it as his own, not as Peter's agent or with permission.

4. Continuous for Statutory Period? YES. 17 years exceeds most state periods (typically 7-20 years). Quagmire used seasonally (consistent with vacation property type—not necessary to live there year-round for seasonal properties). Seasonal use qualifies as continuous for property type.

5. Adverse (Without Permission)? YES. Peter never gave permission—he didn't even know. Quagmire's use is presumed adverse. Peter's inattention and ignorance don't defeat the adverse claim; burden is on Peter to show he gave permission (he can't).

CONCLUSION: Quagmire likely acquires title through adverse possession. Peter loses the property despite formal ownership because he failed to exercise dominion and control. This illustrates adverse possession's policy: creates incentive to actively use and monitor property; rewards productive use; prevents idle holdings from blocking development.

Why This Matters: Classic adverse possession scenario showing how law punishes inattentive owners and rewards those who invest in land improvement. Tests all five elements in realistic fact pattern.

5. Trespass: Enforcing the Right to Exclude

Foundational Concept: Right to Exclude

The right to exclude is "one of the most essential sticks in the bundle of rights." It is enforced through trespass doctrine. A trespasser is someone who intentionally enters or remains on land without permission.

Elements of Trespass:

  1. Intentional (knowing or reckless) entry or remaining on land
  2. Without permission/privilege
  3. On land of another

Remedies:

  • Damages (actual damages for harm; nominal damages if no harm)
  • Injunction (order to stop trespassing)
  • Self-help (reasonable removal of trespasser—but limited by law)

State v. Shack (1971) — Right to Exclude vs. Right of Access

Doctrine: Limits on Right to Exclude; Public Policy Exception

Facts: Anti-poverty group members entered Shack's farm (without permission) to provide legal and medical services to migrant farmworkers living on his property. Shack had posted no-trespassing signs and warned them away. They returned. Shack had them arrested for trespass.

Holding: Trespass conviction reversed. Despite Shack's ownership and right to exclude, certain individuals (social workers, attorneys) can enter private property to provide necessities of life to vulnerable people living there.

Key Principles:

  • Right to exclude is not absolute
  • Public policy limits property rights when fundamental needs at stake
  • Vulnerable populations (migrant workers) may need outside assistance
  • Owner cannot monopolize access to people on his property when they need basic services
  • State interest in ensuring welfare of vulnerable persons overrides property right

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Fundamental case on limits of property rights and intersection of property law with public policy. Tests understanding that property rights are not absolute and can yield to compelling state interests. Frequently tested in essays asking about balancing property rights against public policy.

Critical Issue—Homelessness & Right of Access: Related doctrine: Homeless persons cannot be prosecuted for trespass in certain contexts. Some jurisdictions recognize right to sleep in public (Martin v. Boise, 2019—not in casebook but reflects modern trend). State v. Shack suggests property rights yielding to ensure basic necessities.

Commonwealth v. Magadini — Limits on Trespass Doctrine

Facts: Similar anti-poverty trespass case.

Holding: May be inconsistent with Shack depending on jurisdiction. Some courts are more protective of property owners' right to exclude.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Shows jurisdictional variation in balancing property rights vs. public policy.

Jacobs v. Steenberg Homes (1997) — Nominal Damages for Trespass

Doctrine: Trespass Remedies and Protecting Property Rights

Facts: Mobile home dealer wanted to move a mobile home across Jacobs' property (shortest route). Jacobs refused permission. Dealer moved it anyway (brief, minimal trespass). No actual damage resulted.

Holding: Jury awarded nominal damages ($1) but also punitive damages (significant amount). Court affirms punitive damages are appropriate to protect fundamental property right to exclude, even when actual harm is zero.

Key Principles:

  • Right to exclude is so fundamental that even minor trespass warrants remedy
  • Don't need actual harm to recover damages
  • Nominal damages ($1) possible for technical trespass
  • Punitive damages available to punish intentional, flagrant violations
  • Purpose: Deter future trespass; protect fundamental right

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Key case on remedies and protection of property rights. Shows that trespass law protects the right itself, not just compensation for harm. Tests understanding of different damage types: nominal, actual, punitive.

Policy Rationale: Property rights are foundational to legal system. If owners can't control who enters, property becomes meaningless. Even technical violations warrant remedy to preserve the right itself.

Pop Culture Hypothetical: South Park — Kyle's Anti-Homeless Fence

Kyle Broflovski builds a homeless shelter on his property and requires homeless persons to live in a fenced area. The shelter provides minimal services: no heat, no medical care, no legal aid. Outside volunteers (social workers from Denver) want to enter the fenced area to provide medical care and conduct legal intake to help homeless persons apply for benefits. Kyle posts no-trespassing signs and threatens arrest. The volunteers enter anyway to provide services.

Kyle (Property Owner): "This is my property! These people are trespassing! I should be able to exclude anyone!"

Volunteers (Social Workers): "These vulnerable people need medical care and legal help. Kyle is preventing them from accessing necessities!"

ANALYSIS: This directly tests State v. Shack doctrine.

Kyle's Right to Exclude Argument: Kyle owns the property and has right to control who enters. Volunteers lack his permission and are technically trespassing. Under pure property law, Kyle could have them arrested.

Shack's Public Policy Exception: However, Shack suggests that when vulnerable people lack access to basic necessities, state interest in their welfare overrides property right. Homeless persons in Kyle's facility need medical care and legal assistance. If Kyle prevents them from accessing these services, he's effectively monopolizing their access (they can't leave to get help, they're dependent on him).

Key Distinction: Shack applies most strongly when: (1) occupants are vulnerable/dependent, (2) services are truly necessary (medical, legal), (3) occupants cannot access services otherwise (landlocked on property). Here all three apply.

Likely Outcome: Court would likely protect volunteers' right to enter under Shack doctrine. Kyle's property right to exclude yields to state interest in ensuring basic welfare of homeless residents. Volunteers may enter to provide necessary services despite Kyle's objection.

Why This Matters: Tests limits of property rights in modern context. Shows that absolute control is not protected when fundamental needs at stake. Forces consideration of competing values: property rights vs. human welfare.

6. Right to Exclude vs. Right of Access: Public Policy Limits

Foundational Principle: Property Rights Have Limits

While property owners have strong right to exclude, this right is limited by public policy considerations. Courts balance property rights against public access in certain contexts.

PruneYard Shopping Center v. Robins (1980) — Free Speech on Private Property

Doctrine: State Constitutional Right of Access to Private Property

Facts: Shopping center owner prohibited patrons from engaging in free speech activities (leafletting) on his private property. California Constitution was interpreted to protect free speech even on private property in certain contexts.

Holding: Private property owners do not have absolute right to exclude persons exercising free speech rights if state constitution protects those rights.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests limits on right to exclude and intersection with constitutional law. Shows property rights yield to fundamental rights in certain contexts.

Uston v. Resorts International (1982) — Casino Access

Doctrine: Public Accommodations Doctrine

Facts: Casino excluded card counter (skilled gambler using mathematical advantage). He sued claiming unjust exclusion from public accommodation.

Holding: Public accommodations (open to general public for commercial purposes) have limited right to exclude customers arbitrarily. Casinos cannot exclude based on protected class (race, etc.) but can exclude based on behavior or gaming policy.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Shows distinction between truly private property and public accommodations. Once you open property to public, right to exclude becomes limited by anti-discrimination law.

7. Nuisance: Interference with Use and Enjoyment

Private Nuisance Doctrine

Definition: Substantial and unreasonable interference with another person's use and enjoyment of their land.

Elements:

  1. Defendant's Conduct: Intentional, negligent, or from abnormally dangerous activity
  2. Substantial Interference: Not trivial; affects reasonable person's use/enjoyment
  3. Use and Enjoyment: Affects claimant's property use (health, comfort, safety, economics)
  4. Unreasonableness: Balancing test:
    • Gravity of harm to claimant (how bad is interference?)
    • Utility/value of defendant's conduct (how important is what defendant doing?)
    • Social/economic value of defendant's activity
    • Feasibility of abatement (can defendant stop without great burden?)
    • Character of neighborhood (industrial vs. residential)

Standard: Unreasonableness is context-specific; courts balance competing interests. Not bright-line rule.

Remedies:

  • Injunction (order to stop nuisance)
  • Damages (compensation for harm)
  • Abatement (self-help: fix the problem yourself)

Boomer v. Atlantic Cement (1970)

Doctrine: Balancing Gravity of Harm vs. Utility; Injunction vs. Damages

Facts: Cement plant operates near residential neighborhoods. Plant emits cement dust, air pollution, vibrations. Plaintiffs (neighboring homeowners) suffer property damage, health issues, diminished property values.

Holding: Court finds nuisance (interference IS substantial and unreasonable). However, court denies injunction (would shut down major industrial employer) and awards damages instead.

Key Holding: When gravity of harm is substantial BUT utility/economic value of defendant's conduct is high, court may award damages rather than injunction. This is called "equity balancing."

Reasoning:

  • Gravity of harm to plaintiffs: Significant (property damage, health impacts, reduced property values)
  • Utility of defendant's conduct: Very high (plant employs hundreds, serves regional economic interests)
  • If court issued injunction: Plant would close, employees lose jobs, regional economy damaged
  • Damages compensate plaintiffs but preserve defendant's ability to operate

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Fundamental case on nuisance remedies. Tests understanding that courts balance competing interests and that remedy (damages vs. injunction) depends on policy analysis. Frequently tested in essays.

Criticism: Damages may inadequately compensate—plaintiffs still live near polluting plant, still suffer harm. Injunction might be more appropriate to protect fundamental right to peaceful enjoyment. But practical concern about shutting down major employer is real.

Alternative Rule: Injunction with Mitigation: Some courts would issue injunction but allow defendant to pay damages to avoid closure (allow purchase of neighboring properties or pollution controls).

Prah v. Maretti (1982) — Solar Access Case

Doctrine: Modern Nuisance Doctrine; Protecting Benefical Uses

Facts: Prah installs solar panels on his roof. Neighbor Maretti proposes building house that would shade Prah's panels (blocking solar access). Prah sues for nuisance.

Holding: Court recognizes nuisance even though Maretti's conduct is "natural" (building on his own land). Shadows from buildings can constitute nuisance when they interfere with solar panel function.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Modern application of nuisance doctrine. Tests understanding that nuisance law evolves with new technologies and policy priorities (renewable energy). Shows courts willing to recognize new types of interference as nuisance.

Policy Rationale: Solar energy serves important public policy goal (environmental protection, energy independence). Prah's investment should be protected even though neighbor's conduct is lawful use of his own property.

Fontainebleau Hotel v. Forty-Five Twenty-Five (1959) — Solar Blockage

Doctrine: Right to Use Own Property vs. Duty Not to Interfere

Facts: New hotel built, casts shadow on neighboring hotel's pool and beachfront. Neighbor sues for nuisance.

Holding: No nuisance. Property owner has right to use his property even if it blocks neighboring property's light/sun. No general "right to light" (unlike some European jurisdictions).

Distinction from Prah: Why does Prah get solar access protection but Forty-Five Twenty-Five doesn't? Key difference: Prah involves beneficial, non-wasteful use (renewable energy) while Forty-Five Twenty-Five involves competing economic interests (hotels competing for same amenities). Some courts give environmental uses higher priority.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that "right to light" is not absolute. Context matters: beneficial use vs. wasteful use; public policy vs. private interest.

Coming to the Nuisance Defense

Doctrine: If claimant moved next to nuisance, can claimant recover?

General Rule: "Coming to the nuisance" is NOT a complete defense (you don't have to tolerate nuisance even if you moved there). However, it may reduce damages because claimant knew what they were getting into.

Exception: In some jurisdictions, if claimant "came to nuisance" (moved to area with pre-existing nuisance), claimant has limited remedy.

Modern Trend: Courts disfavor "coming to nuisance" defense; claimant still has right to enjoy property even if nuisance pre-existed.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of defenses to nuisance claims and equitable doctrines limiting remedies.

Pop Culture Hypothetical: Rick and Morty — Portal Research Laboratory Nuisance

Rick Sanchez builds a portal gun research laboratory in his garage at 123 Morty Lane. The lab emits loud noises at all hours (portal gun testing creates sonic booms) and toxic fumes (chemicals used in portal technology). Fumes drift onto neighbor Jerry's property, killing his prized rose garden, and contaminating his well water. Jerry suffers insomnia from the noise. Jerry sues Rick for private nuisance.

Rick: "I'm conducting cutting-edge scientific research that benefits humanity! My work is important!"

Jerry: "Your lab is destroying my property and ruining my sleep!"

ANALYSIS OF NUISANCE ELEMENTS:

1. Defendant's Conduct? YES. Rick intentionally operates the laboratory. The noise and fumes are natural and foreseeable results of portal gun research.

2. Substantial Interference? YES. Noise at "all hours" causes insomnia. Toxic fumes kill garden plants and contaminate well water. These are substantial interferences—not trivial or minor.

3. Use and Enjoyment? YES. Jerry's use and enjoyment of his property is affected: inability to sleep (health), dead roses (aesthetic enjoyment), contaminated water (health, use of property). All three dimensions of "use and enjoyment" affected.

4. Unreasonableness—Balancing Test:

  • Gravity of Harm: Significant. Insomnia affects Jerry's health. Dead roses are property damage. Contaminated well water is serious health hazard. Harm is substantial.
  • Utility of Rick's Conduct: Portal gun research is scientifically interesting but not essential to community welfare (unlike cement plant serving regional economy). Rick can't show public benefit.
  • Social/Economic Value: Portal gun research has speculative benefit ("benefits humanity" is vague). Compare to Boomer: Cement plant employed hundreds and served known regional need. Rick's lab employs only Rick (and Morty, unwilling participant).
  • Feasibility of Abatement: Rick can abate nuisance by: (1) soundproofing lab (feasible), (2) installing fume extraction systems (feasible), (3) moving lab away from residential area (possible), or (4) limiting research hours (feasible). Solutions exist without shutting down research.
  • Neighborhood Character: Residential neighborhood (Jerry's roses, well water indicate residential property). Rick's industrial-scale research is out of place in residential area.

UNREASONABLENESS CONCLUSION: Rick's conduct is unreasonable. Gravity of harm is substantial. Utility is modest/speculative. Abatement is feasible without ending research. Neighborhood is residential, not industrial.

5. Remedy: Should court issue injunction or award damages?

Case 1 (Following Boomer): Court recognizes nuisance but awards damages. But here, unlike Boomer, defendant isn't major employer. Injunction wouldn't cause significant economic harm. Court likely issues injunction requiring Rick to: (1) soundproof lab, (2) install emissions controls, or (3) cease operations.

Case 2 (Balancing Equity): If Rick could show community benefit, court might award damages instead. But given modest nature of research and feasibility of abatement, injunction more likely.

Comparison to Boomer: Unlike Boomer's cement plant (major employer, serves regional economy, abatement would require closure), Rick's lab can implement controls without shutting down. Injunction is appropriate remedy.

Why This Matters: Tests full nuisance analysis including gravity/utility balancing, remedy selection, and context-specific analysis. Shows how courts weigh competing interests and choose remedies accordingly.

8. Easements—Express: Creating Property Rights Over Another's Land

Easement Doctrine: Fundamental Concept

Definition: Right to use another's land for specific, limited purpose without possessing it. Easement holder has right to use; owner of servient land still owns but must allow use.

Components of Every Easement:

  • Dominant Estate: Land that benefits from easement (benefited by right to use)
  • Servient Estate: Land burdened by easement (has someone else's use rights)
  • Easement Holder: Person/entity with right to use servient estate
  • Burden Holder: Owner of servient estate

Express Easements: Created by Written Grant

Requirements:

  1. Writing (Statute of Frauds)
  2. Clear intent to create easement
  3. Identify dominant and servient estates
  4. Describe scope/purpose of use

Types of Express Easements:

  • Easement Appurtenant: Easement benefits land (dominant estate)
    • Benefit runs with dominant estate to successors
    • If dominant estate sold, buyer gets easement benefit
    • Burden runs with servient estate (if recorded)
    • Example: Right-of-way across neighbor's land to access your property
  • Easement in Gross: Easement benefits person, not land
    • Benefit does NOT automatically run to successors
    • If holder sells, assignee may not have right (depends on jurisdiction)
    • Usually not assignable unless explicitly authorized
    • Example: Utility company's right to run power lines (easement in gross)

Green v. Lupo — Express Easement and Scope

Facts: Grant of easement for "driveway purposes." Later, easement holder tries to use for commercial parking.

Holding: Scope of express easement is limited to purpose stated in grant. Cannot expand use beyond stated purpose without consent.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that easement scope is limited by grant language. Changes in use require consent or new easement.

Cox v. Glenbrook Co. — Easement Appurtenant vs. In Gross

Facts: Utility company granted "right to maintain and operate electric lines."

Holding: Easement in gross (benefits utility company as entity, not specific dominant estate). Company can transfer rights to successor utility companies.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests distinction between appurtenant (runs with land) and in gross (tied to person/entity). Shows how utility easements are typically in gross.

9. Easements—Prescriptive, By Estoppel, and Implied

Prescriptive Easement: Acquiring Through Adverse Use

Doctrine: Similar to adverse possession but for easements (right to use, not right to possess).

Elements (similar to AP but key differences):

  • Open and Notorious: Use visible to reasonable owner (same as AP)
  • Continuous for Statutory Period: Same as AP (varies by state)
  • Adverse (Without Permission): Same as AP
  • KEY DIFFERENCE: NOT exclusive. Prescriptive easement holder doesn't exclude owner from land; owner can still use land alongside easement holder

Outcome: Easement holder acquires easement for limited use, not full possession.

Compare to Adverse Possession: AP requires exclusive possession; prescriptive easement does not. Both require adverse use for statutory period.

Easement by Estoppel: Reliance Creates Right

Doctrine: If owner permits use and holder relies on reliance in reasonable way, court recognizes easement.

Elements:

  1. Owner permits use (express or implied)
  2. Holder relies on permission (reasonable reliance)
  3. Holder invests money/effort based on reliance
  4. Owner tries to revoke permission after reliance

Remedy: Court recognizes easement to prevent injustice from reliance.

Example: Neighbor orally permits you to use driveway. You pave driveway at great expense. Neighbor tries to revoke. Court recognizes easement by estoppel.

Lobato v. Taylor — Easement by Estoppel and Community Context

Facts: Rural community context; neighbor permits use of path.

Holding: Easement by estoppel recognized. Community context and reliance show intent to create permanent easement.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that context matters (rural vs. urban) and that reliance can create easement without formal written grant.

Implied Easement from Prior Use

Doctrine: When land severed, prior use may create easement obligation on new owner.

Elements:

  1. Servient and dominant estates FORMERLY ONE PARCEL (under common ownership)
  2. AT TIME OF SEVERANCE, continuous apparent use showed servient/dominant relationship
  3. Use was:
    • Apparent (visible, not hidden)
    • Continuous (regular pattern)
    • Permanent (not temporary)
  4. Continuation is "reasonably necessary" to enjoyment (important: not "absolutely necessary")
  5. Before severance, no intent to terminate use

Application: Land owned by one person; has driveway across eastern portion. Owner divides land, sells western half to buyer. Driveway across western half essential for eastern portion. Court recognizes implied easement.

Rationale: Original owner wouldn't intend severance to destroy usefulness. Buyer takes with knowledge of prior use (constructive notice). Efficiency: maintains land's utility.

Granite Properties v. Manns — Implied Easement

Facts: Prior use evident from land conditions.

Holding: Implied easement recognized. Reasonably necessary (but not absolutely) for dominant estate. Preserves original owner's intent.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that easements can be created by implication from prior use without written grant.

Easement by Necessity: Strict Requirements

Doctrine: Courts recognize easement when land becomes landlocked (no access to public road).

Elements (strict):

  1. Dominant and servient estates FORMERLY ONE PARCEL
  2. AT SEVERANCE, dominant became landlocked (no access)
  3. NO OTHER ADEQUATE ACCESS exists

Based on Implied Intent: Grantor wouldn't intend to render land worthless/unsellable.

Complications: If easement must cross third-party land (not original servient estate), courts hesitate (creates externality on innocent third party).

Judicial Reluctance: Courts rarely grant easement by necessity. Very high bar. Extreme circumstances required.

Frech v. Piontkowski — Implied Easement and Necessity

Facts: Land severed; one parcel became landlocked or severely inconvenienced.

Holding: Easement recognized based on implied intent or necessity.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of how courts create easements to preserve land's value and utility.

Easement Termination

Ways Easement Ends:

  • Express Agreement: Parties agree to terminate
  • Time-Limited Grant Expires: If stated to end at certain time
  • Merger: One person owns both dominant and servient estate
  • Abandonment: Clear evidence of intent to abandon (non-use alone insufficient)
  • Adverse Possession Against Easement: Servient owner adversely possesses easement holder
  • Frustration of Purpose: Purpose becomes impossible (rare)
  • Non-Re-Recording: Some states—easement lapses if not re-recorded after 30-50 years

10. Concurrent Tenancies: Co-Ownership and Rights/Duties

Types of Co-Ownership

1. TENANCY IN COMMON:

  • Each owner owns separate, undivided interest
  • Interests CAN be of unequal value
  • Share passes to heirs at death (devisable)
  • No survivorship rights
  • Each tenant can:
    • Transfer their interest without others' consent
    • Partition (force sale and divide proceeds)
    • Possess entire property (but liable for rent to others)
  • Contribution rules: One tenant pays more than fair share can seek reimbursement

2. JOINT TENANCY:

  • Each owner owns entire property (collectively)
  • FOUR UNITIES required:
    • TIME: Acquired at same time
    • TITLE: From same source/instrument
    • INTEREST: Equal interests
    • POSSESSION: Equal right to possess entire property
  • IF ONE UNITY MISSING: Becomes tenancy in common
  • KEY FEATURE: SURVIVORSHIP RIGHTS
    • At death, survivor takes entire property
    • Deceased's share doesn't pass to heirs
    • Property passes outside probate
  • Can be severed: Voluntary sale, involuntary sale (foreclosure), or death
  • If one joint tenant transfers: Becomes tenancy in common (unity of time broken)

3. TENANCY BY THE ENTIRETY:

  • Only for married couples
  • Similar to joint tenancy (with survivorship)
  • Can't be unilaterally severed (even by one spouse)
  • Both spouses must agree
  • Creditor of one spouse can't attach (protected from separate debts)
  • Death of spouse: Survivor takes entire property

Carr v. Deking — Concurrent Tenancy Rights and Disputes

Facts: Co-tenants dispute rights to property and rent collection.

Holding: Rights of co-tenants defined by tenancy type. Tenancy in common gives each right to possess and share rent equally.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of different rights/duties under different tenancy types.

Tenhet v. Boswell — Joint Tenancy Severance

Facts: One joint tenant transfers their interest to third party. Question: Does this sever joint tenancy?

Holding: Yes. Transfer by one joint tenant breaks unity of time and title, creating tenancy in common.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that joint tenancy is fragile and easily severed by one co-tenant's transfer.

Sawada v. Endo (1977) — Tenancy by the Entirety and Creditor Protection

Doctrine: Tenancy by Entirety as Judgment-Proof Arrangement

Facts: Husband and wife own home as tenants by the entirety. Husband injured someone in accident; creditor obtains judgment against husband personally. Creditor tries to attach home to satisfy judgment.

Holding: Creditor cannot attach home. Tenancy by the entirety protects property from claims of single spouse's creditors. Both spouses must consent to encumber property.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that tenancy by the entirety offers creditor protection that other tenancy types don't. Shows how property law can protect marital assets.

Policy Rationale: Family home should be protected from creditors of one spouse. Both spouses have equal interest; one can't encumber without other's consent.

Rights and Duties of Co-Tenants

POSSESSION:

  • Each co-tenant has right to possess entire property
  • Cannot exclude other co-tenants (unless they don't pay rent)
  • Tenant in possession can use property but may owe rent to others
  • Ouster: Deliberate exclusion requires accounting for fair value

RENT/SHARING:

  • If co-tenant collects rent from third party: May owe to others (divided fairly)
  • BUT: Rent from third parties sometimes kept by collecting tenant (varies by jurisdiction)

CONTRIBUTIONS:

  • Repairs and maintenance: Can claim reimbursement from other tenants
  • Taxes and mortgage: Can force contribution or claims at partition
  • Voluntary improvements: Usually no right to reimbursement

PARTITION:

  • Either co-tenant can force partition (sale and division of proceeds)
  • Physical partition: Divide property (if equal division possible)
  • Judicial partition: Court sale, proceeds divided

Pop Culture Hypothetical: South Park — The McDonald's House Purchase

Stan and Kyle purchase a commercial property (former McDonald's restaurant) together. Stan contributes $100,000; Kyle contributes $50,000. They take title as "Stan Smith and Kyle Broflovski, as tenants in common." The property generates $30,000/year in rent from commercial tenant. Stan lives in the back office and runs the property. Kyle never comes to the property.

Years later, Kyle needs money and wants to sell his interest to an investor. Stan refuses to sell. Kyle threatens to force partition. Stan argues Kyle should owe him money for his contributions to property upkeep.

Stan: "I put in 2/3 of the capital and I'm maintaining the property! You should owe me! And I don't consent to partition—I want to keep the property!"

Kyle: "We agreed to be co-tenants! That means I have the right to force partition or sell my interest! I'm doing it!"

ANALYSIS OF TENANCY IN COMMON RIGHTS:

Capital Contribution: Stan contributed 2/3, Kyle 1/3. However, in tenancy in common, unequal contributions don't create unequal ownership rights (unlike partnership). Stan still owns 1/2 and Kyle 1/2 (unless written agreement said otherwise).

Rent Distribution: $30,000/year rent should be distributed equally: $15,000 to Stan, $15,000 to Kyle. If Stan collected rent and kept all of it, Kyle can claim share. Stan's argument that he's maintaining property doesn't entitle him to extra rent—maintenance is separate issue.

Maintenance Contributions: If Stan paid for repairs/maintenance out of his pocket beyond his share, he can likely claim reimbursement from Kyle. Maintenance benefits entire property, so costs are shared.

Transfer Right: Kyle has absolute right to transfer his 1/2 interest to investor. Stan cannot prevent this. Transfer creates new co-tenancy between Stan and investor. Stan's consent is not required.

Partition Right: Kyle can force partition (sale of property and division of proceeds). Stan cannot prevent this. Partition is fundamental right of co-tenants. Stan must either: (1) buy out Kyle's interest at fair value, or (2) submit to judicial sale.

Outcome: Kyle wins. Kyle has right to: (1) transfer interest to investor, (2) force partition if sale is only way to divide property. Stan's remedy is to buy out Kyle's interest if he wants to keep the property. Stan can also recover share of rent if Kyle wasn't paid, and can claim reimbursement for maintenance costs he paid.

Why This Matters: Tests understanding of tenancy in common rights and limitations. Shows that co-tenants have strong rights (transfer, partition) that can override other co-tenants' preferences. Shows how unequal capital contributions don't create unequal ownership or control.

11. Covenants & Equitable Servitudes: Binding Future Owners

Foundational Problem

Parties make promise about land use. What happens when property is sold to new owner? Does new owner have to follow old promise? How far into the future can you control land use?

Real Covenants (Law): Strict Elements

All five elements REQUIRED for covenant to bind successors:

1. WRITING (Statute of Frauds)

  • Covenant must be in writing
  • Part of deed or separate agreement

2. INTENT TO BIND SUCCESSORS

  • Original parties must intend future owners bound
  • Usually shown in writing

3. TOUCH AND CONCERN THE LAND

  • Covenant must relate to property value/use
  • Not purely personal promise
  • Test factors:
    • Does it affect property value?
    • Impose burden on land interest?
    • Increase value of other land?
    • Affect rights related to property?

4. HORIZONTAL PRIVITY (between original parties)

  • Usually: Sale of land between original parties
  • Instantaneous privity (transfer at same time)
  • NOT: Mere agreement without land transaction

5. VERTICAL PRIVITY (between successor and predecessor)

  • Strict privity: Successor takes ALL property rights
  • Relaxed privity: Successor takes some interest (leasehold)
  • Varies by jurisdiction

REMEDY: DAMAGES (legal remedy, not injunction).

Equitable Servitudes: Relaxed Elements, Powerful Remedy

Definition: Covenant enforced in equity (injunction) rather than law (damages).

Elements (relaxed compared to real covenants):

  1. WRITING (Statute of Frauds)
  2. INTENT TO BIND SUCCESSORS
  3. TOUCH AND CONCERN THE LAND
  4. NOTICE TO SUCCESSORS (not privity)
    • Actual: Direct knowledge
    • Constructive: Properly recorded
    • Inquiry: Facts on ground suggest interest (should investigate)

REMEDY: INJUNCTION (stop violation, not just compensate with damages).

Neponsit Property Owners' Association v. Emigrant Industrial Savings Bank (1938)

Doctrine: Affirmative Covenants Run to Successors; HOA Fees

Facts: Planned community with CCRs (Covenants, Conditions, Restrictions) requiring residents to pay annual fees to HOA. Property transferred to bank; bank refuses to pay fees.

Holding: HOA fees are covenant that "touches and concerns" land. Burden runs to successors. HOA can enforce fee collection against successor.

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Fundamental case on covenants and HOA law. Marks shift from traditional doctrine (affirmative duties now run). Tests understanding that modern law recognizes covenants can bind future owners.

Significance: Enables planned communities to function. HOA can enforce rules and collect fees from all owners, not just original covenantees.

Nahrstedt v. Lakeside Village Condominium Association (1994)

Doctrine: Judicial Review of HOA Covenant Enforcement; Deferential Standard

Facts: HOA covenant prohibits pets. Nahrstedt owns cat; HOA enforces covenant, fines her.

Holding: HOA covenant enforced with deferential review. CCRs presumed valid and consistent. Court defers to HOA's reasonable interpretation.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that courts defer to HOA judgment in enforcing covenants. Shows HOA has power to enforce reasonable restrictions.

El Di, Inc. v. Town of Bethany Beach — Equitable Defenses

Doctrine: Changed Circumstances Defense to Covenant Enforcement

Facts: Covenant restricts commercial use in residential area. Neighborhood changed dramatically (now commercial zone). Enforcement would be pointless.

Holding: Changed circumstances defense available. Equitable defenses protect against unfair enforcement.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that even valid covenants can be blocked by equitable defenses.

Equitable Defenses to Covenant Enforcement

Covenant runs to successors, BUT equity provides defenses:

  • Changed Circumstances: Neighborhood changed so dramatically that original purpose impossible
  • Relative Hardship: Burden on defendant >> benefit to plaintiff; court balances equities
  • Acquiescence: Previous violations tolerated for long period; implies covenant no longer enforced
  • Abandonment: So many violations in neighborhood; implies covenant is abandoned
  • Unclean Hands: Plaintiff also violating covenant; can't enforce against others
  • Estoppel: Association said "won't enforce"; successor relied on this
  • Laches: Unreasonable delay in enforcing; defendant detrimentally relied
  • Prescription: Statute of limitations ran on breach

Pop Culture Hypothetical: Family Guy — Quahog HOA Enforcement

Peter Griffin lives in Quahog Estates, a planned community with CCRs requiring: (1) front lawns be kept to 2-3 inches, (2) exterior paint colors limited to earth tones, (3) no political signs in yards, (4) HOA fees of $200/month. Peter violates every rule: grass is 8 inches (he has a sheep), painted house bright orange, has giant "Vote for Giggity" sign, and hasn't paid HOA fees in 6 months.

The HOA Board (including Ernie the Chicken) issues warnings, then fines Peter. Peter refuses to comply or pay. HOA seeks injunction and lien on property. Peter sues, claiming CCRs are unenforceable.

HOA Board: "These covenants are clear! Everyone else follows them! Peter is destroying property values!"

Peter: "This is my property! I can paint it orange if I want! And I shouldn't have to pay $200/month to keep grass short!"

ANALYSIS OF COVENANT ENFORCEABILITY:

Real Covenant Test for HOA Fees:

  • Writing? YES. CCRs are written in deed.
  • Intent to Bind Successors? YES. Explicitly states restrictions bind future owners.
  • Touch and Concern? YES. HOA fees directly affect property value and use.
  • Horizontal Privity? YES. Original developer and first owners had privity when property sold with covenants.
  • Vertical Privity? YES. Peter took title; privity exists.

RESULT: HOA fees enforceable. Peter must pay $200/month or face lien.

Equitable Servitude Test for Aesthetic Restrictions (grass, paint, sign):

  • Writing? YES.
  • Intent? YES.
  • Touch and Concern? YES. Affects property values and neighborhood appearance.
  • Notice? YES. CCRs recorded; Peter had constructive notice. Or: Inquiry notice (if area is well-maintained planned community, should investigate).

RESULT: Restrictions enforceable through injunction.

Can Peter Raise Equitable Defenses?

  • Changed Circumstances? NO. Neighborhood hasn't changed; still planned community with aesthetic restrictions.
  • Relative Hardship? Possibly. If HOA fees are crushing (example: $200/month is 30% of Peter's income), court might reduce rather than enforce. But unlikely to eliminate entirely.
  • Acquiescence? Only if HOA ignored violations for years without objection. If HOA just now starting enforcement, defense fails.
  • Abandonment? Only if most residents violating covenants. If Quahog Estates is well-maintained and Peter is outlier, defense fails.
  • Unclean Hands? Only if HOA officials also violated covenants while enforcing against Peter. Unlikely.

Likely Outcome: HOA prevails. Peter must: (1) pay back HOA fees, (2) comply with aesthetic restrictions (mow grass, paint house approved color, remove sign), and (3) pay ongoing fees. Courts defer to HOA under Nahrstedt standard.

Why This Matters: Tests complete covenant doctrine including real covenants (for fees), equitable servitudes (for aesthetic rules), and equitable defenses. Shows how modern HOA law enforces restrictions and enables planned community management.

12. Marital Property: Ownership Rights Between Spouses

Two Systems: Community Property vs. Common Law

COMMUNITY PROPERTY STATES (California, Texas, Arizona, others):

  • Property acquired during marriage = community property (owned 50-50)
  • Earned income during marriage = community property
  • Separate property: Before marriage or after divorce
  • At divorce: Community property divided equally
  • Policy: Recognizes both spouses' contributions (earning and homemaking)

COMMON LAW PROPERTY STATES (Including most Eastern states like Massachusetts where BU is located):

  • Marital property controlled by title alone
  • Whoever has title owns property
  • Creates inequality (earning spouse vs. homemaking spouse)
  • Remedies: Alimony, equitable distribution at divorce, other statutory protections

Wilbur v. DeLapp — Property Rights and Domestic Partnerships

Facts: Unmarried couple cohabits; one partner contributes labor while other contributes money. On breakup, dispute over property division.

Holding: Some states recognize property rights between unmarried partners based on unjust enrichment or implied contract.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that property rights can exist between unmarried partners depending on jurisdiction and agreements.

Tompkins v. Jackson — Cohabitation and Property Claims

Facts: Long-term cohabitation; one partner claims property interest in other's assets.

Holding: Depends on agreement and reliance. Some jurisdictions recognize claims; others don't.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Shows variation in how courts treat nonmarital partners' property claims.

Nonmarital Partners: Growing Recognition

Recent Doctrine: Growing recognition of non-marital cohabiting couples' rights.

  • Some states recognize property rights between unmarried partners
  • Unjust enrichment claims
  • Implied agreements
  • Estoppel (reliance on cohabitation arrangement)
  • Statutory registration (some states)

Policy Issues: Who counts as "family"? Should law recognize cohabitation as creating property rights? Different approaches by different states. Trend toward greater recognition (following marriage equality).

13. Present Estates & Future Interests: Property Over Time

Present Estates: Current Possessory Rights

Freehold Estates (ownership-like interests):

  • Fee Simple (absolute ownership): Can last forever; inheritable; freely transferable; most complete property interest. Can be modified by conditions.
  • Fee Simple Subject to Condition Subsequent: Has condition ("If land used for residential only..."). If condition violated: Original grantor can reclaim. Right to reclaim = Power of termination (held by grantor).
  • Fee Tail (largely obsolete): Passes only to direct descendants. Eventually becomes fee simple.
  • Life Estate: Lasts only for someone's life (life tenant's or another's). Non-inheritable. Ends at death. Life tenant has right to possess and use; must maintain property (not waste); cannot unilaterally transfer beyond their life.
  • Life Estate Pur Autre Vie: Lasts during another person's life. Ends when that person dies.

Leasehold Estates (possession for term):

  • Discussed separately in landlord-tenant section

Future Interests: Rights to Property Later

Reversion: Right held by grantor after granting lesser estate. If grant: "To A for life" = grantor retains reversion. At A's death, property returns to grantor (or heirs).

Remainder: Right held by third party when lesser estate ends. If grant: "To A for life, then to B" = B has remainder interest. Vested remainder: Holder is ascertainable and no conditions precedent. Contingent remainder: Holder unknown or condition precedent.

Executory Interest: Right to take property (divest another's interest) in future. Can be held by third party. Different from remainder (shifts property between parties).

Rule Against Perpetuities: No interest can be contingent on event that might not occur within lives in being plus 21 years. Prevents dead hand control forever. Complex doctrine (typically 1L Property doesn't cover deeply). Future interests course covers extensively.

Wood v. Board of County Commissioners — Present Estates and Transfer

Facts: Grant of land "for so long as used for public park." Question: What estate is created?

Holding: Conditional fee (fee simple subject to condition). If use ceases, grantor can reclaim.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding of conditional estates and how language affects property interest created.

14. Landlord-Tenant Law: Leasing Property and Tenant Rights

Foundational Concept: Leasehold Estate

Tenant has possession for defined period. Landlord retains reversion (property returns at lease end). Different from owned property (tenant has limited rights). Tenant bound by lease terms; landlord bound by law.

Lease Formation

Requirements:

  1. Writing (Statute of Frauds for leases > 1 year)
  2. Parties: Landlord (lessor) and tenant (lessee)
  3. Premises identified
  4. Term (duration) specified
  5. Rent/consideration
  6. Intent to create landlord-tenant relationship

Lease Agreement: Contract between parties setting forth rights/duties. Governed by contract law AND property law.

Subleasing vs. Assignment

SUBLEASE: Tenant transfers part of lease term (not entire remaining term)

  • Tenant retains reversionary interest
  • Subtenancy created between original tenant and subtenant
  • Original landlord and subtenant have no direct relationship
  • BUT: Original landlord may have right to restrict (lease terms)

ASSIGNMENT: Tenant transfers entire remaining lease term

  • Tenant has no reversionary interest
  • Direct landlord-tenant relationship between landlord and assignee
  • Privity of estate exists

LANDLORD'S RIGHT TO RESTRICT:

  • Many leases require landlord consent to assignment/sublease
  • "Reasonable consent may not be withheld" standard (in some states)
  • Conflicts between business interests of landlord/tenant

Kendall v. Ernest Pestana (1985) — Landlord Consent to Assignment

Doctrine: Landlord Cannot Unreasonably Withhold Consent to Assignment

Facts: Tenant seeks to assign lease to new tenant. Lease requires "landlord consent" but says nothing about reasonableness.

Holding: Landlord cannot unreasonably withhold consent. Implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing requires reasonableness.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that law implies obligations even when lease is silent. Shows balance between landlord's right to control property and tenant's right to transfer.

Constructive Eviction

Doctrine: Landlord's material breach allowing tenant to vacate without liability for remaining rent.

Elements:

  1. LANDLORD'S MATERIAL BREACH (failure to provide essential services: habitability, utilities, major repairs)
  2. TENANT LEAVES PREMISES in response (must actually vacate)
  3. WITHIN REASONABLE TIME (must leave promptly after breach)
  4. NO RESPONSIBILITY FOR BREACH (breach must be landlord's fault, not tenant's)

Remedies:

  • Tenant can vacate without paying remaining rent
  • Tenant can sue for damages (past rent, relocation costs)
  • Does NOT give tenant ownership of property

Policy: Encourages landlords to maintain premises. Protects tenants from uninhabitable conditions. Allows exit without breach of contract.

Minjak Co. v. Randolph — Constructive Eviction Doctrine

Facts: Residential property becomes uninhabitable (no heat, water problems). Tenant wants to vacate without paying rent.

Holding: Constructive eviction available. Tenant can vacate and recover damages.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Shows how law protects tenants from uninhabitable conditions through constructive eviction doctrine.

Warranty of Habitability

Doctrine: Implied warranty that residential premises are suitable for human occupation.

Modern Law: Most states imply warranty; common law did not.

Applies to: Residential leases (NOT commercial). Premises must be habitable for normal residential purposes. Basic requirements: Roof, walls, windows, utilities, heat, sanitation, etc.

Cannot Be Waived: Landlord cannot force tenant to sign waiver. Public policy requires habitability regardless of lease terms. Protects vulnerable tenants.

Breach Occurs When: Conditions fall below habitability standard. Examples: No heat, broken roof, infested with pests, no running water, lead paint. Objective standard: Would normal person find uninhabitable?

Tenant's Remedies:

  • Repair and Deduct (some states): Tenant makes repairs, deducts cost from rent (subject to limits)
  • Withhold Rent: Pay into escrow pending repair (must follow procedure)
  • Breach of Warranty Defense: If evicted for non-payment, claim warranty breach as defense
  • Damages: Sue landlord for diminished habitability (often reduced rent for period of breach)

Javins v. First National Realty Corp. (1970) — Implied Warranty of Habitability

Doctrine: Fundamental Shift in Landlord-Tenant Law

Facts: Public housing complex with serious maintenance problems (broken windows, no heat, plumbing failures). Tenants withheld rent pending repairs. Landlord sued for eviction.

Holding: Court recognizes implied warranty of habitability. Landlord must maintain premises in habitable condition. Tenant's failure to pay rent is justified if landlord breaches habitability warranty.

Why This Matters for Exam (HIGH): Landmark case on landlord-tenant law. Fundamental shift from caveat emptor ("buyer beware") to modern consumer protection. Tests understanding that law creates obligations even without written terms.

Policy Rationale: Residential housing is essential to human dignity. Landlords, not tenants, control maintenance. Landlords in better position to make repairs. Protection needed for vulnerable tenants who have limited bargaining power.

Scope: Residential leases only (commercial leases don't have implied warranty—commercial tenants have more bargaining power).

Pop Culture Hypothetical: South Park — Kenny's Apartment Nightmare

Kenny rents a basement apartment from Jimbo (gun club owner) for $300/month. The lease is oral and just says "month-to-month rental." Conditions are terrible: (1) No heat (it's winter; outside temp is 10°F). (2) Roof leaks, causing water damage and mold. (3) Bathroom pipes burst; raw sewage backs up into apartment. (4) Rat infestation. Kenny calls Jimbo repeatedly; Jimbo refuses to fix anything ("It's an old building, deal with it").

After one month, Kenny can't take it anymore. He moves out (goes to Butters' basement as refuge) and refuses to pay the second month's rent. Jimbo sues Kenny for $300 unpaid rent. Kenny claims constructive eviction and warranty of habitability breach.

Jimbo: "Kenny signed a lease! He owes me rent! I'm not responsible if he chose to leave!"

Kenny: "The apartment is uninhabitable! No heat in winter, mold, sewage, rats! I had to leave for health and safety!"

ANALYSIS OF CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION:

1. Landlord's Material Breach? YES. Lack of heat in winter is material breach (health hazard; makes apartment uninhabitable). Water damage/mold is material. Raw sewage is material (health hazard). Rat infestation is material.

2. Tenant Leaves in Response? YES. Kenny vacated the apartment in response to uninhabitable conditions.

3. Within Reasonable Time? YES. After one month, Kenny left promptly when conditions became unbearable.

4. Not Tenant's Fault? YES. None of the conditions resulted from Kenny's actions. Jimbo's failure to maintain caused all problems.

CONSTRUCTIVE EVICTION CONCLUSION: All elements met. Kenny can vacate without paying rent. Kenny can also sue for damages (moving costs, medical expenses from exposure to mold/sewage).

ANALYSIS OF WARRANTY OF HABITABILITY:

Applies to: Residential lease (Kenny's apartment is residential).

Warranty Content: Implied warranty that premises suitable for human occupation. Must have: Roof (no leaks), walls, heat, utilities, sanitation (no sewage backup), freedom from pests.

Breach: All conditions breached:

  • No heat = violates basic habitability requirement
  • Roof leaks + mold = violates requirement for dry, healthy environment
  • Raw sewage = violates sanitation requirement (major breach)
  • Rat infestation = violates pest-free requirement

Objective Standard: Would normal person find apartment habitable? Clearly NO. No reasonable person would live with these conditions.

Tenant's Remedies:

  • Vacate without liability for rent: YES (constructive eviction)
  • Sue for damages: YES (past rent paid for uninhabitable period, moving costs, damages from exposure to hazards)
  • If still in possession, withhold rent: YES (under warranty theory)

Can Jimbo Defend?

  • "Kenny assumed the risk": NO. Warranty cannot be waived (oral lease is just contract; can't override statutory warranty).
  • "Old building": NOT A DEFENSE. Standard is whether habitable, not whether new.
  • "It was Kenny's fault": NO. Kenny didn't cause Jimbo's failure to maintain.

Outcome: Kenny wins. Kenny owes NO rent for the second month. Kenny can recover damages for first month (since conditions existed then too). Total recovery: Rent paid + moving costs + damages for health exposure. Jimbo must make repairs or face liability for future tenants.

Why This Matters: Tests complete understanding of warranty of habitability (Javins doctrine), constructive eviction, and tenant remedies. Shows how law protects vulnerable tenants like Kenny from landlord neglect. Illustrates policy shift toward requiring landlord maintenance responsibility.

15. Fair Housing Act: Anti-Discrimination in Housing

Fair Housing Act (1968): Fundamental Anti-Discrimination Law

Protected Classes:

  • Race/Color
  • Religion
  • National Origin
  • Sex (including gender identity, sexual orientation in some interpretations)
  • Familial Status (families with children)
  • Disability

Prohibited Conduct:

  • Refusal to rent/sell based on protected class
  • Discrimination in terms/conditions
  • Steering (directing people away based on class)
  • Harassment
  • Failure to accommodate (for disability)
  • Discriminatory advertising

Two Types of Discrimination:

  • Discriminatory Treatment: Different treatment based on protected class (explicit or implicit bias)
  • Disparate Impact: Facially neutral policy has disproportionate impact on protected class (e.g., credit score requirements that exclude minorities)

Remedies:

  • Damages (compensatory and punitive)
  • Injunctive relief
  • Civil rights enforcement
  • Attorney fees

Limitations:

  • Owner-occupied buildings (sometimes exempted)
  • Bona fide occupational qualifications (rare)
  • Disability accommodations (reasonable accommodations, not undue burden)

Policy: Ensures equal access to housing. Prohibits private discrimination. Enforces civil rights in property transactions. Addresses historical discrimination (redlining, etc.).

Shelley v. Kraemer (1948) — Covenants and Racial Discrimination

Doctrine: State Action in Private Property Discrimination

Facts: Restrictive covenant on property prohibits sale to non-whites. Seller tries to enforce covenant when buyer (African American family) purchases.

Holding: Courts cannot enforce racially restrictive covenants. While private parties can make covenants, courts' enforcement of them constitutes "state action" violating equal protection (14th Amendment).

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Shows limits on private property rights when they involve discrimination. Tests understanding that law won't enforce discriminatory property arrangements.

Fair Housing Council of San Fernando Valley v. Roommate.com (2012)

Doctrine: Fair Housing Act Liability for Online Platforms

Facts: Online roommate matching service allows users to specify "no minorities," "no people with disabilities," etc. Plaintiffs claim service facilitates discrimination.

Holding: Service liable for discrimination. Even though service doesn't create content, it facilitates discriminatory matching.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Modern application of Fair Housing Act to internet. Shows law reaches private discrimination even in contemporary contexts.

Asbury v. Brougham — Disability Discrimination Case

Doctrine: Reasonable Accommodations for Disability

Facts: Landlord refuses to allow tenant to have service animal (argues "no pets" policy). Tenant has disability requiring service animal.

Holding: Landlord must allow reasonable accommodation (service animal) despite "no pets" policy.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that Fair Housing Act requires reasonable accommodations for disability, overriding property owner's property rules.

Quigley v. Winter — Discriminatory Impact

Doctrine: Disparate Impact Liability Under Fair Housing Act

Facts: Housing selection criteria that appear neutral (credit score, income) have disparate impact on protected class (lower-income minorities).

Holding: Disparate impact discrimination is violation even without discriminatory intent.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that Fair Housing Act covers both intentional discrimination and facially neutral policies with discriminatory effects.

Human Rights Commission v. LaBrie — Age and Familial Status

Facts: Housing provider refuses to rent to families with children (claims property is for "active seniors").

Holding: Familial status is protected class. Cannot exclude families based on children.

Why This Matters for Exam (MEDIUM): Tests understanding that familial status protection prevents discrimination against families with children.

1. Constitutional Interpretation

The Doctrine

Constitutional interpretation is the methodology courts use to determine what the Constitution means. Three major approaches dominate modern jurisprudence:

  • Originalism: The Constitution means what it originally meant when ratified. Courts look to the original public meaning of the text or the Framers' intent. Justices like Scalia and Thomas advocate this approach.
  • Living Constitution: The Constitution evolves with society. Its meaning can change as times and circumstances change, allowing the Constitution to address modern problems. More flexible approach used by progressive justices.
  • Textualism: Focus on the plain language of the text itself, without regard to intent or living meaning. What does the text say? Courts should not read in unstated meanings.

Hybrid Approaches: Modern courts often blend these methods. The debate over proper interpretation methodology appears in nearly every major case.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Prof. Tsai emphasizes constitutional interpretation throughout the course. Understanding these methodologies is essential because they explain how different Justices reach different conclusions from the same text. You must be able to identify which interpretive approach supports a particular argument and critique alternative approaches.

Key Cases

Marbury v. Madison (1803): Chief Justice Marshall used a textualist approach, reading Article III strictly to determine what the Constitution says about federal court jurisdiction. He argued the Constitution must control over conflicting statutes because it is the supreme law.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Rick Sanchez is a brilliant scientist who discovers that the Second Amendment was drafted with only muskets in mind. He argues in federal court that the Second Amendment cannot apply to modern semi-automatic rifles because "arms" in 1791 meant muskets. Morty, a gun rights advocate, counters that "the right to bear arms" means what the text says—arms, period—which naturally includes all weapons available today.

ANALYSIS: This hypothetical pits originalism against textualism. Rick is using original public meaning originalism (the Framers only contemplated muskets). Morty is using a textualist approach (the text says "arms," not "18th-century muskets," so the text is not limited). Under a living constitution approach, courts might ask whether modern firearms are so dangerous that they fall outside historical tradition. This shows how the same constitutional text yields different results depending on interpretive methodology—a core theme of Con Law.

2. Judicial Review and the Countermajoritarian Difficulty

The Doctrine

Judicial Review: The power of courts (particularly the Supreme Court) to strike down laws as unconstitutional.

Origin: Established by Chief Justice Marshall in Marbury v. Madison (1803). Marshall reasoned that: (1) the Constitution is the supreme law; (2) courts must interpret the law they apply; (3) if the Constitution conflicts with a statute, courts must apply the Constitution and declare the statute void.

The Countermajoritarian Difficulty: Judicial review creates a tension with democratic principles. Unelected judges can invalidate laws passed by elected representatives. Why should nine unelected Justices override the will of Congress or the President? This fundamental tension underlies debates about judicial activism vs. restraint.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

The first day of class covers this. It's foundational. You must understand the rationale for judicial review and the tension between judicial power and democratic legitimacy. This theme recurs throughout the course.

Key Cases

Marbury v. Madison (1803): William Marbury was appointed as a justice of the peace but never received his commission. He sued Secretary of State James Madison seeking a writ of mandamus (order to perform a duty). Marshall held that Section 13 of the Judiciary Act of 1789 unconstitutionally expanded federal court jurisdiction beyond what Article III permits. Marbury lost the case, but the Supreme Court established its power to strike down unconstitutional laws. This is THE foundational case.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Congress passes the "Preservation of Family Values Act," which bans all animated shows depicting non-traditional families, citing concerns about children's moral development. Stan Smith from American Dad challenges the law as unconstitutional. The Court must decide: Does the Constitution permit this ban?

ANALYSIS: This raises the countermajoritarian difficulty directly. Congress (the elected representatives of the people) passed this law. But if it violates the Constitution, should the Court strike it down? Following Marbury, if the Constitution conflicts with the statute, the Court must apply the Constitution. The question becomes: Is there a constitutional right at stake (First Amendment? Due Process?), and if so, is the law narrowly tailored to serve a compelling government interest? The tension between the elected branches' policy choice and judicial enforcement of constitutional limits is precisely what Marbury addresses.

3. Separation of Powers and Presidential Power

The Doctrine

The Constitution divides federal power among three branches: legislative (Congress), executive (President), and judicial (courts). Each branch has distinct powers, and the system includes checks and balances.

Presidential Power: The President's enumerated powers are modest: commander-in-chief of military, head of executive branch, treaty-making power (with Senate advice/consent), veto power, and appointment power. But Presidents often claim implied or inherent powers, especially during emergencies.

Limits on Presidential Power: Courts have held that Presidents cannot ignore statutes or the Constitution. In emergencies, Presidents may have broader powers, but courts will scrutinize such claims. The doctrine of "separated powers" prevents concentration of authority.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM

Prof. Tsai covers separation of powers as a structural principle. It's less emphasized than commerce clause or equal protection but remains important for understanding constitutional limits on federal authority.

Key Cases

Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer (1952): During the Korean War, President Truman ordered seizure of steel mills to prevent a strike. The Court held that the President lacked constitutional authority to seize private property without congressional approval. Justice Jackson's concurrence established the framework: (1) maximum presidential power when acting with Congress; (2) twilight zone of concurrent power; (3) minimum presidential power when acting against Congress. This case shows that presidential power is not unlimited.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

President Lois Griffin declares a national emergency due to an alien invasion (in the Family Guy universe). She orders the military to seize all Internet servers and technology companies to coordinate defense, bypassing normal appropriations and contract law. Companies sue, arguing the President exceeded her authority.

ANALYSIS: Under Youngstown, the President's power depends on congressional authorization. Even in emergencies, the President cannot unilaterally seize private property without statutory authority. If Congress authorized emergency seizures, the President would have maximum power. If Congress prohibited it, the President's power is at its lowest. Here, the President acted unilaterally in a twilight zone. The Court would ask: Did Congress implicitly authorize emergency takings? Is this a genuine military necessity? Can the President achieve the goal through other means (like Executive Order under IEEPA or existing statutes)? The case illustrates that emergency powers are real but bounded by law.

4. Federalism and Enumerated Powers: The Necessary and Proper Clause

The Doctrine

The Constitution grants Congress specific enumerated powers (tax, regulate interstate commerce, coin money, etc.). The Necessary and Proper Clause (Article I, Section 8) permits Congress to pass laws necessary and proper to execute these powers.

Three Questions:

  1. Is there a valid enumerated power?
  2. Is the statute rationally related to executing that power?
  3. Does the statute violate any independent constitutional limitation (like the Second Amendment)?

Broad Interpretation: Under McCulloch v. Maryland, the Necessary and Proper Clause is read broadly. Congress has discretion in choosing means to accomplish enumerated ends. The clause extends to implied powers not explicitly listed.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

McCulloch v. Maryland is a cornerstone case. Understanding the Necessary and Proper Clause is essential for federalism questions. It sets the baseline for congressional power before specific limits (like the Commerce Clause limits) apply.

Key Cases

McCulloch v. Maryland (1819): Maryland taxed the Bank of the United States. The Court held that Congress could charter a national bank under the Necessary and Proper Clause, even though banking is not explicitly enumerated. Marshall used a broad interpretive approach: the Clause grants Congress latitude to choose means, and the Clause implies that Congress is the best judge of whether a measure is necessary. Maryland could not tax the bank because the Supremacy Clause prevents states from interfering with federal functions. This case established a presumption in favor of congressional authority and broad construction of the Necessary and Proper Clause.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Congress, concerned about obesity and chronic disease, passes the "Junk Food Tax Act," imposing a federal excise tax on sugar, saturated fats, and processed foods, and using the revenue to fund a national health initiative. Peter Griffin, who loves donuts and chicken wings, challenges the tax as unconstitutional. He argues that regulating food choices exceeds Congress's enumerated powers.

ANALYSIS: Under McCulloch, Congress has the enumerated power to tax. The question is whether a junk food tax is rationally related to an enumerated power. Congress could justify it as: (1) an exercise of the taxing power (Article I, Section 8, Clause 1); (2) a regulation of interstate commerce (food production/sales affect interstate commerce); (3) justified under the Necessary and Proper Clause as a means to reduce healthcare costs and preserve interstate commerce. As long as Congress's choice is rational, the tax survives. McCulloch's broad approach means Congress has wide latitude. Peter's challenge would likely fail unless the tax is purely arbitrary or violates another constitutional limit.

5. Commerce Clause - Early Period: From Gibbons to the Classical Approach

The Doctrine

Commerce Clause: "Congress shall have power to regulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States" (Article I, Section 8, Clause 3).

Gibbons v. Ogden Framework: Chief Justice Marshall defined "Commerce" broadly as intercourse between states. He held that if an activity substantially affects interstate commerce, Congress can regulate it under the Commerce Clause. This broad interpretation set the stage for federal economic regulation.

Early Period (through 1930s): The Court interpreted the Commerce Clause expansively. If an activity involves multiple states or affects interstate trade, it is "commerce among the states" subject to federal regulation.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

The Commerce Clause is one of the most important topics in Con Law. Understanding its historical evolution from Gibbons through the modern era is critical. The exam likely includes Commerce Clause questions.

Key Cases

Gibbons v. Ogden (1824): New York granted a monopoly to operate steamships in New York waters. Gibbons, operating under federal license, challenged the monopoly. Marshall held that the Commerce Clause empowers Congress to regulate navigation and transportation affecting interstate commerce. The scope of federal power is broad—if an activity affects interstate commerce, Congress can regulate it. New York's monopoly conflicted with federal law and was struck down. This case established that federal commerce power is plenary and preempts conflicting state law.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Cartman from South Park starts a cartoonish online business selling novelty items (South Park merchandise) exclusively to customers in Colorado. However, he advertises nationally and ships some items across state lines. Colorado passes a law banning the sale of "frivolous merchandise that promotes satirical mockery of communities." Cartman challenges the law as unconstitutional under the Commerce Clause.

ANALYSIS: Under Gibbons, if Cartman's business affects interstate commerce (shipping across state lines, national advertising, etc.), the Commerce Clause is triggered. The question becomes whether Colorado's ban violates the Commerce Clause. Even if it falls within a state's police powers, it might violate the dormant Commerce Clause (a state cannot discriminate against interstate commerce). Under Gibbons's broad approach, federal power to regulate interstate transactions is substantial. Cartman's challenge has merit because his business involves interstate commerce that Gibbons would clearly reach.

6. Commerce Clause - New Deal Era: Expansion and Transformation

The Doctrine

Historical Context: The Great Depression forced Congress to regulate economic activity to provide relief, recovery, and reform. President Roosevelt's New Deal programs faced constitutional challenges.

Key Cases of the Era:

  • West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937): The "switch in time that saved nine." The Court upheld minimum wage laws, signaling acceptance of economic regulation and deference to Congress.
  • Wickard v. Filburn (1942): The Court upheld federal regulation of wheat production by individual farmers. Even wheat grown solely for personal consumption substantially affects interstate commerce (by reducing demand for wheat sold in interstate markets). This case vastly expanded the reach of the Commerce Clause.
  • U.S. v. Darby (1941): The Court upheld federal wage and hour regulations. Congress can regulate labor practices in manufacturing because they affect interstate commerce in manufactured goods.
  • NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel (1937): Federal labor law applied to manufacturing. Labor practices in production affect interstate commerce.

The Takeaway: By the 1940s, the Court recognized that nearly all economic activity substantially affects interstate commerce, giving Congress broad regulatory authority.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

This era established the baseline of commerce power that persists today. Understanding Wickard's "substantial effects" test is essential. The exam may ask how these cases support or limit federal authority.

Key Cases

Wickard v. Filburn (1942): Farmer Filburn grew wheat for personal use on his farm, beyond his federally-allotted quota. He challenged the Agricultural Adjustment Act as exceeding Congress's power. The Court held that even wheat for personal consumption substantially affects interstate commerce. If Filburn grows his own wheat, he does not buy wheat in interstate markets, reducing demand. Aggregated with other farmers, this significantly affects interstate commerce. Under the "substantial effects" test, Congress can regulate the activity. This established that the commerce power extends to activities that are intrastate and non-commercial if they substantially affect interstate commerce when aggregated.

West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937): Washington state's minimum wage law was upheld. The Court affirmed that Congress and states can regulate working conditions and wages. This marked the end of the Lochner era and acceptance of economic regulation. The Court adopted rational basis review for economic laws—if the law is rationally related to legitimate state goals (worker protection, public health), it survives.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Congress passes the "Animated Beverage Purity Act," regulating the composition of juice drinks and energy drinks sold across state lines, setting standards for sugar content, artificial ingredients, and nutrient fortification. Stan Smith, a bottling company owner, challenges the law. He argues that since he only sells within Virginia, it does not involve interstate commerce.

ANALYSIS: Under Wickard's substantial effects test, the question is whether the bottling business substantially affects interstate commerce. The answer is yes: beverages are bought and sold in interstate markets; regulations on composition affect competition between intrastate and interstate producers; suppliers of ingredients source materials across state lines. This is nearly identical to Wickard—individual economic activity that, when aggregated, substantially affects interstate commerce. Under New Deal precedent, Congress can regulate the activity. Stan's challenge would fail. The key insight is that Wickard transformed the commerce clause to reach local, intrastate economic activity if it has substantial effects on interstate commerce.

7. Commerce Clause - Modern Limits: Lopez, Morrison, and NFIB v. Sebelius

The Doctrine

Post-1990s Retrenchment: After accepting nearly unlimited commerce power for 50 years, the Court began imposing limits on Congress. Three categories emerged:

  1. The Channels of Interstate Commerce: Congress can regulate the channels through which interstate commerce flows (roads, railroads, waterways, Internet).
  2. The Instrumentalities and Things in Interstate Commerce: Congress can regulate the instrumentalities (ships, trucks, planes) and goods moving in interstate commerce.
  3. Activities Substantially Affecting Interstate Commerce: Congress can regulate local activities if they substantially affect interstate commerce. BUT there are limits: the activity must have a rational connection to interstate commerce, and courts will not extend this category indefinitely.

Key Limits:

  • The regulated activity must have a substantial effect on interstate commerce.
  • The statute must contain a jurisdictional element (like "involves interstate commerce") or clear congressional finding.
  • The connection between the regulated activity and interstate commerce must be rational, not attenuated.
  • The regulation cannot be essentially local or intrastate in nature with only remote effects on interstate commerce.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

NFIB v. Sebelius (the Affordable Care Act case) is contemporary and important. Understanding the three categories and the limits of category three is essential. This shows that commerce power, while broad, is not unlimited.

Key Cases

U.S. v. Lopez (1995): Federal law banned guns in school zones. The Court held that the law exceeded Congress's commerce power. The statute lacked a clear jurisdictional element; the regulated activity (gun possession at school) has only a tenuous connection to interstate commerce. This was the first time in 60 years the Court struck down a law as exceeding commerce power. The Court reasoned that if Congress can regulate gun possession in schools, there is no meaningful limit on its power (every local activity remotely affects interstate commerce). The three categories framework emerged here.

U.S. v. Morrison (2000): A provision of the Violence Against Women Act, allowing victims to sue perpetrators in federal court, was struck down. Violence by a private individual against another private individual is not "commerce"; even if it affects interstate commerce (lost work productivity), the connection is too attenuated. This case showed that not all activities with substantial effects qualify—the activity itself must involve commerce or a commercial actor. Non-economic activity (violence) cannot be regulated under the commerce clause.

NFIB v. Sebelius (2012): The Affordable Care Act's individual mandate (requiring purchase of health insurance) was challenged. Chief Justice Roberts, in a narrow majority, held that the mandate exceeded the commerce power in category three. The mandate regulates inactivity (not buying insurance), and the Court was reluctant to uphold a statute regulating inactivity. If Congress can mandate the purchase of insurance, it can mandate purchase of any product, effectively eliminating limits on commerce power. Roberts distinguished Wickard (regulating existing activity) from the mandate (regulating non-activity). HOWEVER, Roberts upheld the mandate as a valid tax under the taxing power. This case shows the modern limits on commerce power: Congress cannot regulate inactivity, and courts remain skeptical of statutes with attenuated connections to commerce.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Congress passes the "Animation Safety Act," requiring all animated characters in television shows distributed across state lines to wear seatbelts in vehicles and to refrain from dangerous stunts without visible safety equipment. The reasoning: animation influences children, children imitate what they see, injuries increase healthcare costs and lost productivity, affecting interstate commerce in healthcare and insurance. Networks broadcast animated shows across state lines, so the statute applies to interstate commerce in broadcasting.

ANALYSIS: This raises the question of whether Congress can regulate animation content under the commerce clause. Under Lopez-Morrison, the three categories are: (1) the statute affects the channels of interstate commerce (broadcasting is clearly interstate), but does it regulate the channel or something else? (2) The statute regulates content, not broadcasting infrastructure. (3) The statute regulates intrastate conduct (character animation) with the claim that it substantially affects interstate commerce through health and safety effects. Under NFIB, courts are skeptical of attenuated causal chains (animation leads to imitation leads to injury leads to healthcare costs). The statute lacks a clear jurisdictional element requiring a nexus to interstate commerce. This statute would likely exceed commerce power. The key principle: Commerce power reaches activity substantially affecting interstate commerce, but the connection must be rational and not purely attenuated through economic chains.

8. Natural Law and the Contract Clause: Fletcher v. Peck and Lochner Era

The Doctrine

The Contract Clause: "No State shall... pass any... Law impairing the Obligation of Contracts" (Article I, Section 10, Clause 1).

Natural Law Background: Early American jurisprudence drew on natural law theory—the idea that certain rights (property, contract) exist prior to government. The Constitution protects these rights.

Fletcher v. Peck (1810): Chief Justice Marshall held that a state cannot rescind a grant of land, even if the grant was procured by fraud. The Contract Clause prevents states from impairing vested property rights. This case reflects natural law thinking: once property is conveyed, the grantee has a vested right that the state cannot take away retroactively.

Lochner Era (1905-1937): The Court struck down economic regulations (minimum wage, maximum hours, price controls) as violating substantive due process and liberty of contract. The Court treated contract freedom as a fundamental liberty. This era ended with West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937).

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM

Fletcher v. Peck is a classic case about judicial authority and constitutional limits on states. The Lochner era is important for understanding the evolution of due process doctrine. Prof. Tsai emphasizes the Lochner problem: how far can courts go in enforcing property rights and liberty of contract against democratic legislation?

Key Cases

Fletcher v. Peck (1810): Georgia granted land to a company; the next legislature, viewing the grant as fraudulent, attempted to rescind it. Marshall held that the grant created a binding contract; the state could not retroactively void it. The Contract Clause protects vested property rights. Even though the original grant was corrupt, the grantee's rights are valid. This case shows judicial enforcement of property rights against state legislatures.

Lochner v. New York (1905): A state law limited bakery workers to 10 hours per day. The Court struck it down as violating liberty of contract (implied in the Due Process Clause of the 14th Amendment). The majority reasoned that the law did not materially promote health and safety; it was a naked exercise of police power. This case became notorious for judicial invalidation of social legislation. The Lochner era lasted until 1937, when West Coast Hotel overruled it, adopting rational basis review for economic laws.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Ted (from the movie "Ted"), a sentient teddy bear, contracts with John Bennett to perform in a comedy act. Later, John wants out of the contract and moves to Massachusetts, which passes a law voiding contracts with non-human entities, arguing that non-human performers violate state labor standards. Ted sues, claiming the law violates the Contract Clause.

ANALYSIS: Under Fletcher v. Peck, the Contract Clause prevents states from impairing vested contractual rights. Ted's contract with John is a binding obligation; Massachusetts cannot retroactively void it. However, the modern doctrine (not followed in Lochner) is that the Contract Clause protects against retrospective legislative impairment of existing contracts, but states retain police power to regulate prospectively. If Massachusetts seeks to prohibit future contracts with non-humans, that might survive as a valid police power exercise. If it voids Ted and John's existing contract, Fletcher says it violates the Contract Clause. This shows the tension between property/contract rights and state legislative authority.

9. Slavery and Secession: Dred Scott v. Sandford and the Road to Civil War

The Doctrine

Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857): Chief Justice Taney held that enslaved people could not become U.S. citizens and had no standing to sue in federal court. Moreover, the Missouri Compromise (which prohibited slavery in territories north of the 36°30' line) exceeded Congress's authority. Taney reasoned that the Fifth Amendment protects slaveholders' property rights in enslaved people; Congress cannot deprive them of this property without due process. This case reinforced slavery and rejected Congressional limits on slavery's expansion.

Constitutional Implications: Dred Scott implied that slavery could not be constitutionally restricted in territories or states, a position that propelled the nation toward civil war. The decision is viewed as one of the worst in Supreme Court history because it failed to recognize the immorality and unconstitutionality of slavery.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM

Dred Scott is a canonical case on the limits of constitutional interpretation and the Court's capacity for error. Prof. Tsai uses it to illustrate how the Court can fail catastrophically. Understanding the case's reasoning and its role in the pre-Civil War political crisis is important for understanding Reconstruction.

Key Cases

Dred Scott v. Sandford (1857): Dred Scott was an enslaved man whose owner took him to a free state and free territory. Scott sued for freedom, arguing he was free by residence in a free jurisdiction. Taney held that Scott was not a citizen (enslaved people were not considered "citizens" at the Founding and remained non-citizens), so he lacked standing to sue. Additionally, the Missouri Compromise violated the Fifth Amendment by depriving slaveholders of property (enslaved people) without due process. This case denied Congressional power to restrict slavery's expansion and protected slavery as property. The decision inflamed Northern opposition and contributed to the election of Lincoln and the Civil War.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

In an alternate history, Rick Sanchez creates sentient robot clones without legal personhood status. The clones demand rights and sue for freedom, but a Court like Taney's holds that they are property, not persons; therefore, they cannot sue. The Court further holds that Congress cannot restrict robot creation on planets in the Andromeda galaxy because slaveholders—excuse me, "creators"—have Fifth Amendment property rights in their robots.

ANALYSIS: This hypothetical mirrors Dred Scott's constitutional logic. By treating sentient beings as property with no legal personhood, courts fail to protect fundamental dignity. The case shows how constitutional interpretation can catastrophically fail when it prioritizes property rights over personhood. Dred Scott is studied as a cautionary tale: courts must be willing to recognize evolving understandings of rights and personhood, or they risk endorsing atrocities. The hypothetical illustrates why the Constitution, though protecting property, cannot permit property rights to override the fundamental rights of sentient, conscious beings.

10. Reconstruction Amendments: The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments

The Doctrine

13th Amendment (1865): "Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime, shall exist within the United States." This abolished slavery nationwide.

14th Amendment (1868): The most important amendment. It contains several clauses:

  • Citizenship Clause: "All persons born or naturalized in the United States... are citizens of the United States." This reversed Dred Scott.
  • Privileges or Immunities Clause: "No State shall abridge the privileges or immunities of citizens of the United States."
  • Due Process Clause: "Nor shall any state deprive any person of life, liberty, or property without due process of law." This applies due process to states (incorporation doctrine).
  • Equal Protection Clause: "Nor deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws." This is the basis for all equal protection doctrine.

15th Amendment (1870): "The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." This outlawed racial discrimination in voting.

Historical Context: These amendments were designed to protect freed slaves and establish equality. However, the Supreme Court quickly limited their scope through cases like the Slaughterhouse Cases and Cruikshank, restricting federal power to enforce them.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

The Reconstruction Amendments are foundational to the entire equal protection and due process doctrine. Understanding their original purpose and how courts have interpreted them is essential.

Key Cases

Slaughterhouse Cases (1873): Louisiana granted a monopoly to a slaughterhouse company. Other butchers sued, claiming their right to engage in trade was violated. The Court held that the Privileges or Immunities Clause does not protect such rights; it only protects rights flowing from national citizenship (voting, interstate travel). State-granted privileges remain subject to state regulation. This narrow reading of the 14th Amendment limited its scope.

U.S. v. Cruikshank (1876): White mob members killed Black citizens exercising voting rights. The Court held that the 14th Amendment does not protect voting rights from private interference; it only restricts state action. Congress cannot punish private discrimination. This case established the state action doctrine—the 14th Amendment applies only to government, not private conduct.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

A state passes a law prohibiting "artificial beings" (robots, AI) from political participation or voting. The AI collective sues, arguing that the 15th Amendment's guarantee of equal voting rights applies. The state responds that the Amendment only protected formerly enslaved people and their descendants.

ANALYSIS: The text of the 15th Amendment does not limit protection to formerly enslaved people; it protects against denial of voting rights "on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude." If AI are "persons" within the meaning of the Amendment, and the state denies them voting rights (even if not explicitly based on race), the question becomes whether the Amendment protects non-racial classifications. The original purpose was racial equality, but modern doctrine treats the Amendment as embodying a principle of voting equality. Courts would need to decide if AIs qualify as "citizens" (14th Amendment Citizenship Clause) and "persons" with voting rights. If yes, then discrimination against them violates the Amendment. If no, then the Amendment does not apply. This shows how constitutional text, read broadly, can protect rights beyond its original contemplation.

11. State Action Doctrine: Cruikshank, Slaughterhouse Cases, and Civil Rights Cases

The Doctrine

State Action Requirement: The 13th and 14th Amendments apply only to government action, not private conduct. If a private person discriminates, the Amendments do not directly apply. Congress can regulate private discrimination through the Commerce Clause or the 13th Amendment (which applies to private slavery), but the 14th Amendment's equal protection guarantee requires state action.

Why State Action?: The 14th Amendment says "No State shall..." implying that its protections apply when states act. Private discrimination is not state action, so it falls outside the Amendment's scope.

Consequences: This doctrine limits the reach of civil rights protections. Congress must use its commerce and other enumerated powers to reach private discrimination, rather than relying directly on the 14th Amendment.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

State action is a threshold requirement for all equal protection and due process claims. Understanding when action is attributable to the state vs. private is essential.

Key Cases

U.S. v. Cruikshank (1876): White mobs massacred Black citizens in Louisiana. Federal prosecutors charged them under Reconstruction-era civil rights laws. The Court held that the 14th Amendment does not punish private violence; it only restricts state action. The 1st and 2nd Amendments already apply to states through the 14th Amendment, but only against state action. Since the defendants were private individuals (not state actors), the Amendment did not apply. Congress could punish them only if they were acting under color of state law. This case severely limited Reconstruction protections.

Civil Rights Cases (1883): Congress passed the Civil Rights Act of 1875, prohibiting private discrimination by hotels, railroads, and theaters. The Court struck it down, holding that Congress cannot prohibit private discrimination under the 14th Amendment (which requires state action). Congress might reach such discrimination under the Commerce Clause (by regulating interstate commerce in lodging and transportation), but the 14th Amendment alone does not empower such regulation. This case created the state action doctrine as we know it.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Kyle from South Park is Jewish. He goes to a private country club in South Park that has a policy: "No Jews admitted." The club is entirely private. Kyle sues under the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. The club argues that it is private, not a state actor, so the 14th Amendment does not apply.

ANALYSIS: Under the state action doctrine (Cruikshank, Civil Rights Cases), the 14th Amendment applies only to state action. A private club is not a state actor; its discrimination is not subject to equal protection. HOWEVER, Congress has power under the Commerce Clause to prohibit private discrimination by places of public accommodation. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits discrimination in hotels, restaurants, and theaters based on race, color, religion, and national origin. So Kyle could sue under the Civil Rights Act (which reaches private entities engaged in interstate commerce), but not under the 14th Amendment directly. The state action doctrine limits the reach of constitutional protection to state action, but Congress can regulate private conduct under other powers.

12. Equal Protection - Segregation Era: Plessy v. Ferguson and Separate But Equal

The Doctrine

Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): The Court upheld a Louisiana law requiring racial segregation on trains. The Court held that the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause permits separate facilities for different races as long as they are "equal." This is the "separate but equal" doctrine.

Rationale: The Court viewed segregation as not implying inferiority; separation did not violate equality as long as facilities were comparable. In reality, separate facilities were almost never equal, and segregation itself conveyed the message that one race was inferior.

Impact: Plessy legitimated Jim Crow segregation for 58 years until Brown v. Board overruled it. The case illustrates how courts can endorse social hierarchy through ostensibly neutral doctrines.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Plessy is the canonical case on wrong constitutional interpretation. Brown explicitly overruled it. Understanding Plessy's reasoning and why it failed is essential to understanding equal protection doctrine.

Key Cases

Plessy v. Ferguson (1896): Homer Plessy, a light-skinned man with African ancestry, was arrested for sitting in a white car on a segregated train. He challenged Louisiana's segregation law as violating the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. The Court upheld the law, reasoning that the Amendment requires equal protection, not identical treatment. Separate but equal facilities satisfy equality. The Court implicitly treated racial segregation as a neutral sorting mechanism, not evidence of subordination. Justice Harlan's dissent argued that the Constitution is colorblind; laws that classify by race inherently violate equal protection. Harlan's view prevailed over a century later in Brown.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

The town of South Park, Colorado establishes separate but equal animated character schools: one for human characters (Cartman, Kyle, Stan) and one for non-human characters (Butters, who is a dog-person, and others). Both schools have identical budgets, facilities, and curricula. The town argues this is equal protection—separate but equal.

ANALYSIS: Under Plessy, if facilities are truly equal, segregation survives. But Plessy is overruled by Brown, which held that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." Segregation itself conveys a message of inferiority and violates equal protection. Moreover, it is virtually impossible to maintain truly equal facilities when one group is excluded from mainstream society. Under modern doctrine, classifications based on race (or analogous immutable characteristics) trigger strict scrutiny and must serve compelling government interests through narrowly tailored means. Classification by species might trigger strict scrutiny (analogous to race) or rational basis (not analogous). Either way, segregation fails. The key lesson: Plessy's separate but equal doctrine was wrong; segregation is inherently unequal.

13. Equal Protection - Desegregation: Brown v. Board I & II

The Doctrine

Brown v. Board of Education I (1954): The Court overruled Plessy, holding that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." Segregation in public schools violates the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. Chief Justice Warren reasoned that segregation generates feelings of inferiority that impede educational opportunity. Modern psychological and sociological evidence showed that segregation harms Black students. Even if facilities are technically equal, segregation itself is unconstitutional.

Brown v. Board II (1955): The Court addressed remedies, holding that desegregation must proceed "with all deliberate speed." This vague standard led to massive resistance and minimal integration for years.

Key Principles:

  • Racial classifications are inherently suspect and trigger strict scrutiny.
  • Segregation violates equal protection even if facilities are equal.
  • Education is a fundamental right or at least particularly important.
  • Remedies for constitutional violations must be broadly tailored to eliminate the violation.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Brown is the watershed case in equal protection doctrine. It overruled Plessy and established that racial classifications trigger strict scrutiny. Understanding Brown's reasoning and its limitations is critical.

Key Cases

Brown v. Board of Education I (1954): Black students in Topeka, Kansas were denied admission to white schools and assigned to segregated Black schools. The Court held that segregation in public schools violates the 14th Amendment. Warren's opinion emphasized that education is fundamental and that segregation impedes equal educational opportunity. Relying on psychological evidence (the famous "doll study" showing that segregation harms Black children's self-esteem), the Court concluded that separate is inherently unequal. Plessy was overruled.

Brown v. Board II (1955): The Court addressed implementation, ordering desegregation to proceed "with all deliberate speed." This famously vague standard allowed massive resistance. Schools were slow to integrate, and the Supreme Court had to order specific remedies (busing, etc.) to enforce desegregation.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

The town of South Park maintains separate schools for human and non-human characters. The town argues that the non-human school is technologically advanced and well-funded, equal or superior to the human school. Butters (a dog-person student) challenges the segregation as violating equal protection. The town responds that this is not race-based but species-based, and equal facilities satisfy the Constitution.

ANALYSIS: Under Brown, even if the non-human school is superior, segregation itself violates equal protection if it is based on a suspect classification (race or analogous characteristic). The question is whether species is analogous to race. If so, segregation triggers strict scrutiny and likely fails because it cannot serve a compelling interest in a narrowly tailored way. Even if species is not a suspect classification (triggering only rational basis), the segregation conveys stigma and inferiority that harms educational opportunity. Brown established that segregation is inherently unequal regardless of facility quality. The town would need to provide a compelling reason for segregation, which it likely cannot articulate. The case would be resolved under Brown's principles: segregation violates equal protection when it stigmatizes students and impedes equal opportunity.

14. Equal Protection - Race and National Origin: Korematsu and Strict Scrutiny

The Doctrine

Strict Scrutiny Standard: Classifications based on race (and analogous suspect characteristics like national origin) trigger strict scrutiny. The government must prove that the classification: (1) serves a compelling government interest and (2) is narrowly tailored to achieve that interest.

Korematsu v. United States (1944): The Court upheld the internment of Japanese-American citizens during World War II, applying strict scrutiny but finding that national security is a compelling interest and internment is necessary to achieve it. This case illustrates how strict scrutiny can still result in upholding restrictions on fundamental rights.

Modern Strict Scrutiny: Contemporary courts are skeptical of race-based classifications. Nearly all race-conscious measures fail strict scrutiny (e.g., affirmative action programs face intense scrutiny).

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Understanding strict scrutiny as applied to race and national origin is essential. Korematsu shows that strict scrutiny can uphold restrictions, but modern cases show skepticism toward race-conscious government action.

Key Cases

Korematsu v. United States (1944): After Pearl Harbor, President Roosevelt issued an Executive Order mandating internment of Japanese and Japanese-American citizens. Fred Korematsu, an American citizen, violated the internment order and was prosecuted. The Court upheld the internment, applying strict scrutiny but finding that the war emergency and concerns about national security constitute a compelling interest. The internment was narrowly tailored to address these concerns. The case is notorious for upholding mass detention based on race/ethnicity. Modern scholars and jurists view it as wrongly decided; strict scrutiny should have struck down the internment because less restrictive alternatives existed (individualized screening rather than blanket internment). The case demonstrates that even strict scrutiny can result in constitutional violations when courts defer to government claims of emergency.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

Following an alien invasion by a species resembling Martians, the federal government orders internment of all residents of Martian descent. The government argues that national security requires this measure because some aliens support invasion. A Martian-American citizen sues, challenging the internment as violating equal protection. The government applies the Korematsu framework.

ANALYSIS: Under strict scrutiny, the government must prove a compelling interest and narrow tailoring. National security is compelling, but narrow tailoring requires the government to use the least restrictive alternative. Blanket internment of all Martian-Americans is not narrowly tailored because it applies to citizens who pose no threat and could be screened individually. Courts today would likely reject the internment. Unlike Korematsu, which courts now view skeptically, modern strict scrutiny would require evidence of actual threat, not mere suspicion based on national origin. The case illustrates the difference between the deferential Korematsu reasoning and modern strict scrutiny, which imposes real limits on race/ethnicity-based classifications.

15. Proving Discriminatory Intent: Washington v. Davis

The Doctrine

Washington v. Davis (1976): A disparate impact (unequal effects) is not enough to establish a constitutional equal protection violation. The government action must be motivated by discriminatory intent or purpose. This is a high bar: facially neutral laws that have a discriminatory effect survive unless the plaintiff proves discriminatory intent.

Proof of Discriminatory Intent: Plaintiffs can show intent through:

  • Discriminatory language in the statute or legislative history.
  • Circumstantial evidence that the law was designed with discriminatory effect in mind.
  • Evidence of impact combined with absence of legitimate explanation.
  • Deviation from neutral selection criteria (e.g., changing the rule only in ways that harm a protected group).

Why This Standard?: The Court feared that disparate impact liability would make nearly every policy vulnerable to challenge if it affected groups differently. The discriminatory intent requirement provides a limiting principle.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Washington v. Davis sets the evidentiary standard for proving equal protection violations. Understanding how to prove discriminatory intent is essential for equal protection questions.

Key Cases

Washington v. Davis (1976): African-American applicants were rejected from the Washington D.C. police force at a higher rate than white applicants. The applicants challenged the Written Personnel Test (WPT) as discriminatory. Statistical evidence showed that the test had a disparate impact on Black applicants. However, the Court held that disparate impact alone does not establish an equal protection violation. The test was not facially discriminatory; it applied equally to all races. To succeed, plaintiffs must prove that the test was adopted with discriminatory intent. The Court found no evidence of intentional discrimination (the test was designed to measure basic reading and writing skills, not to exclude Blacks). Therefore, the test survived. This case established that equal protection requires discriminatory intent, not just disparate impact.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

South Park's police department adopts a new hiring exam that includes a question: "What is your favorite beverage?" The test is designed to identify whether applicants appreciate Kyle's family's cultural heritage. Statistically, Jewish applicants score 95% pass rate, while all other groups score 20% pass rate. Non-Jewish applicants sue under the 14th Amendment.

ANALYSIS: Under Washington v. Davis, disparate impact alone does not establish an equal protection violation. The question is facially neutral (asks about beverages); it does not explicitly classify by religion or ethnicity. The fact that Jewish applicants pass at much higher rates is circumstantial evidence of discriminatory intent. However, plaintiffs must prove that the question was adopted with discriminatory purpose. If South Park's police chief created the question intending to favor Jewish applicants, then it violates equal protection. If the question was adopted for a legitimate non-discriminatory reason (e.g., it genuinely measures relevant skills), then the disparate impact does not trigger strict scrutiny. Plaintiffs would need to show intent through direct evidence (discriminatory statements, departure from normal procedures, or a pattern of deviation from race-neutral criteria). This shows how Washington v. Davis makes it difficult to prove discrimination without explicit evidence of intent.

16. Race and Criminal Justice: US v. Clary and Disparities in Sentencing

The Doctrine

Crack-Powder Sentencing Disparity: Federal law imposed much harsher penalties for crack cocaine possession than powder cocaine possession. Possession of 5 grams of crack triggered a 5-year mandatory minimum; possession of 50 grams of powder cocaine triggered the same penalty. This 100:1 ratio had a disparate racial impact because Black defendants were prosecuted for crack offenses while white defendants were prosecuted for powder offenses.

US v. Clary (1994): A Black defendant challenged the sentencing disparity as violating equal protection. The Court held that the defendant must prove discriminatory intent by the legislature (Congress) in adopting the sentencing scheme. Disparate impact alone is insufficient. Even though the sentencing scheme had a severe disparate racial impact, without proof that Congress intentionally intended to discriminate, the scheme survived. The defendant could not meet the high burden of proving Congressional intent.

Reform: In 2010, Congress passed the Fair Sentencing Act, reducing the crack-powder ratio to 18:1, acknowledging the disparity. But the constitutional challenge in Clary failed because Washington v. Davis requires proof of intent.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM

Clary illustrates the practical application of Washington v. Davis to criminal sentencing. It shows how facially neutral sentencing schemes can have disparate racial impact but survive equal protection challenge absent proof of intent. This is an important limit on equal protection doctrine.

Key Cases

United States v. Clary (1994): A Black defendant convicted of crack cocaine possession challenged the 100:1 sentencing disparity as violating equal protection. He presented evidence that the disparity had a severe disparate racial impact: Black defendants received sentences 6-7 times longer than white defendants for equivalent drug amounts. However, he could not prove that Congress intentionally designed the disparity to harm Black defendants. The Court held that equal protection requires proof of discriminatory intent. Citing Washington v. Davis, the Court found that despite the stark disparate impact, the facially neutral sentencing scheme did not violate equal protection. This case shows the difficulty of proving equal protection violations in criminal law absent proof of intent. Even severe disparities can survive if facially neutral.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

A town passes an ordinance imposing $1000 fines for playing "aggressive rap music" in public but only $100 fines for playing "country music." Statistically, 80% of rap music violators are Black residents, while 80% of country music listeners are white. A Black defendant charged under the ordinance challenges it as discriminatory.

ANALYSIS: Under Washington v. Davis and Clary, the question is whether the ordinance is facially discriminatory or whether it was enacted with discriminatory intent. The ordinance does not facially classify by race; it classifies by music genre. The severe disparate impact (80% of people fined are Black) is circumstantial evidence of intent, but not conclusive. The defendant must prove that the town council intended to discriminate against Black residents. If the ordinance was designed to deter "aggressive music" without regard to race, it might survive. If the council explicitly discussed targeting Black residents or chosen "rap" as a proxy for race, that would show intent. The difficulty is proving intent without direct evidence. Clary shows that even ordiances with stark disparate racial impact can survive if facially neutral and unadopted with explicit intent. However, the combination of facial classification (by music genre that correlates with race) and disparate impact might trigger heightened scrutiny as discriminatory on its face or in its application.

17. Levels of Scrutiny: Rational Basis, Intermediate, and Strict Scrutiny

The Doctrine

Three Tiers of Scrutiny: Courts apply different levels of review depending on the classification or right at stake.

Rational Basis Review (Most Deferential):

  • Applied to ordinary economic and social regulations.
  • Standard: The government action must be rationally related to a legitimate government interest.
  • Burden: The challenger must prove the law is irrational, an almost impossible task.
  • Outcome: Almost all laws survive rational basis review.
  • Rationale: Courts defer to legislative judgments on economics and social policy; democratic processes provide sufficient check.

Intermediate Scrutiny (Middle Ground):

  • Applied to classifications based on gender and some other characteristics (legitimacy, sexual orientation in some contexts).
  • Standard: The government action must serve an important governmental objective and be substantially related to achieving that objective.
  • Burden: Moderate burden on government; more searching than rational basis but less strict than strict scrutiny.
  • Outcome: Some laws survive; others fail.
  • Rationale: Some classifications are inherently suspect but not as dangerous as race; more scrutiny than rational basis is warranted.

Strict Scrutiny (Most Stringent):

  • Applied to laws classifying by race, national origin, alienage, and laws affecting fundamental rights (voting, interstate travel, First Amendment freedoms).
  • Standard: The government action must serve a compelling government interest and be narrowly tailored to achieve that interest.
  • Burden: Heavy burden on government; rarely upheld.
  • Outcome: Most laws fail strict scrutiny.
  • Rationale: Race and fundamental rights are core constitutional concerns; judicial protection is necessary.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: HIGH

Understanding levels of scrutiny is fundamental to all equal protection and due process analysis. Every hypo requires identifying the correct tier of scrutiny and applying the appropriate test.

Key Cases

Williamson v. Lee Optical (1955): A law prohibited opticians from fitting and duplicating eyeglasses without a prescription from an ophthalmologist. Opticians challenged the law. The Court applied rational basis review and upheld the law, finding it rationally related to protecting public health and vision care standards. The case exemplifies how rational basis review is nearly impossible to fail.

Craig v. Boren (1976): Oklahoma prohibited sale of beer to males under 21 and females under 18. The Court applied intermediate scrutiny to gender classifications. It held that the different ages did not substantially relate to preventing drunk driving (the stated objective) because teenage female drivers were not significantly more likely to drink and drive than teenage males. The law failed intermediate scrutiny.

Brown v. Board (1954): Racial segregation in schools fails strict scrutiny. Even if separate facilities are theoretically equal, they do not serve a compelling interest and are not narrowly tailored. Segregation stigmatizes students and harms educational opportunity.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

South Park passes three laws: (1) an ordinance requiring restaurants to post calorie counts (affects everyone equally—rational basis); (2) a law limiting drinking ages for men to 21 but women to 18, citing different physiologies (gender classification—intermediate scrutiny); (3) a law prohibiting "non-human characters" from voting in school board elections (race or species analogue—strict scrutiny?). Each is challenged.

ANALYSIS: Law 1: Rational basis. Calorie posting rationally relates to public health. Easily survives. Law 2: Intermediate scrutiny. Gender classifications are suspect. Is different drinking ages substantially related to an important governmental objective? Not really; physiological differences between sexes do not vary dramatically by one year, and individual variation within each sex is huge. Likely fails intermediate scrutiny (following Craig v. Boren). Law 3: If "non-human characters" constitutes a suspect class (analogous to race), strict scrutiny applies. Voting rights are fundamental. Can the town show that excluding non-humans serves a compelling interest? Probably not. The law likely fails strict scrutiny. If non-humans are not a suspect class, rational basis applies, and the law might survive if rationally related to some interest (though it's hard to articulate one). This hypothetical shows how the correct tier of scrutiny determines the outcome.

18. Economic Liberty and the Modern Welfare State: Lochner and Beyond

The Doctrine

Lochner Era (1905-1937): The Court struck down economic regulations as violating substantive due process and liberty of contract. The Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments protect liberty of contract from unjustified government intrusion.

End of Lochner (1937-Present): West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937) marked the end of heightened protection for economic liberty. The Court adopted rational basis review for economic regulations. As long as a law is rationally related to a legitimate government interest, it survives.

Modern Doctrine:

  • Economic liberty receives minimal constitutional protection (rational basis review).
  • Legislatures have broad discretion to regulate economic activity, working conditions, prices, entry into professions, etc.
  • There is no fundamental right to engage in economic activity free from regulation.
  • The Court defers to legislative judgments about economic policy; judges do not second-guess political choices.

Exception: If a law affects fundamental rights (like voting or speech) or suspect classifications (like race), heightened scrutiny applies even if the law regulates economic activity.

Why It Matters for the Exam

Exam Likelihood: MEDIUM

Understanding the evolution from Lochner to modern rational basis review for economic regulations is important. It shows how constitutional interpretation changes and how courts can overrule prior precedent.

Key Cases

Lochner v. New York (1905): A state law limited bakery workers to 10 hours per day. The Court struck it down as violating substantive due process. The majority reasoned that liberty of contract (implied in the Due Process Clause) protects the right to work longer hours if desired. The law did not materially promote health and safety; it was a naked exercise of police power. Justice Holmes's dissent criticized the majority for enforcing its laissez-faire economic philosophy. This era lasted until West Coast Hotel overruled it.

West Coast Hotel v. Parrish (1937): Washington's minimum wage law was upheld. The Court adopted a deferential rational basis standard for economic regulations. As long as the law is rationally related to a legitimate state interest (worker protection, public health, etc.), it survives. This case ended Lochner and established that economic liberty is not fundamental; legislatures have broad discretion to regulate.

Pop Culture Hypothetical

A state passes a "Cartoon Safety Act" limiting animated character employment to 8 hours per day and requiring safety equipment. Mr. Burns from The Simpsons (who employs many animated characters) challenges the law as violating his liberty of contract and property rights.

ANALYSIS: Under Lochner-era reasoning, liberty of contract would protect Mr. Burns's right to employ characters without hour limits. But Lochner is overruled. Under modern doctrine, the state can regulate working conditions under rational basis review. The law is rationally related to legitimate interests in worker safety and preventing exploitation. Mr. Burns's constitutional challenge fails. The state has broad discretion to regulate employment conditions; there is no fundamental right to unlimited employment of workers. This shows how West Coast Hotel displaced Lochner and now economic regulation receives minimal constitutional scrutiny.
